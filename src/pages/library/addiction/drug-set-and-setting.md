## About This Book

*Drug, Set, and Setting* (Yale University Press, 1984) is Norman E. Zinberg's landmark study of controlled intoxicant use. Drawing on interviews with hundreds of drug users, Zinberg demonstrated that the pharmacological properties of a substance alone cannot explain patterns of use, dependence, or abstinence. Instead, three factors interact:

- **Drug** — the pharmacological action of the substance itself.
- **Set** — the mindset of the user: personality, mood, expectations, and prior experience.
- **Setting** — the social and physical environment in which use occurs, including informal social controls, rituals, and sanctions.

Zinberg showed that many users of illicit substances — including heroin and psychedelics — maintained controlled, non-compulsive use over extended periods when the social setting provided appropriate informal sanctions and rituals. The book challenged then-dominant assumptions that any use of certain drugs inevitably leads to addiction.

The framework has become foundational in harm-reduction theory and continues to inform drug-policy research and practice. For a related empirical study applying a similar framework to heroin use in the Netherlands, see the companion resource on [drug use as a social ritual](/library/harm-reduction/drug-use-as-a-social-ritual/description-of-the-study) by J.P. Grund.
