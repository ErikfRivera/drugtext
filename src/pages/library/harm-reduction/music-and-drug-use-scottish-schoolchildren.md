*Alasdair Forsyth & Marina Barnard. Centre for Drug Misuse Research, University of Glasgow, 11 the Square, Glasgow G12 8RT. Paper presented at the IHRC Paris harm-reduction conference proceedings.*

## Abstract

Drug use data were collected from three samples of Scottish children (n = 3928) between the springs of 1996 and 1997. This data collection took place at a time of great media interest in the possible influence that drug use by pop stars might be having over their teenage fans. To see if there was any evidence for such a relationship these teenage respondents' favourite type of music was compared against their personal use of illegal drugs. The results presented here confirm earlier findings that rave is the style of music most popular with teenage drug users. There was little evidence to indicate that fans of the pop stars currently being criticised in media are likely to be drug users. However, there was some evidence to suggest that children who identify with pop music acts from the late 1960s to mid 1970s are likely to be drug users.

## Introduction

It is commonly accepted that there is a connection between drug use and popular music. There have been reports of drugs being used in association with many styles of music, ranging from primitive tribal drum-beats to the sophisticated electronic music of the 1990s (Lyttle and Montague, 1992; Saunders, 1995). In some recent sub-cultures drugs have been used as an accompaniment to music, such as ecstasy with the dance music of 1990s (Collin, 1997). In others, music has been used to enhance the drug experience, such as the LSD acid tests of the 1960s (Stevens, 1987).

Today however, this linkage between music and drugs has tended to be framed in terms of the influence that drug using pop stars have over impressionable adolescents. In the view of the media, and many influential others, the relationship between music style and whether or not it is compatible with the effects of drugs is not seen as the most important issue. Instead, their concerns have focused on the drug using behaviours of popular musicians and pop songs with drug-related lyrics. The inference being that if pop stars sing about drugs and are honest about using them then their young fans are sure to follow suit (*Newsnight*, 1997; *the Scotsman*, 1997; *Sunday Mail*, 1997). In recent years stories in the press concerning the use of drugs by teenage pop idols have been legion. Examples, noted at the time of data collection for this paper, include stories breaking about stars in the pop groups Oasis (*Daily Record*, 1997a), Blur (*Daily Record*, 1996), East 17 (*Evening Times*, 1997) and Take That (*Glasgow Herald*, 1996). These stories were often bolstered by drawing attention to the past (and apparently much greater) drug related misfortunes of 1960s pop acts such as the Beatles, Pink Floyd, the Doors, Jimi Hendrix, the Rolling Stones and the Who (*Sunday Mail*, 1996).

In recent months these concerns have culminated in calls for songs with drug related lyrics to be banned. On an edition of the BBC current affairs programme *Newsnight* (1997) a senior police officer and prominent anti-drug campaigner singled out songs by the groups Pulp (Sorted for Es and wiz) and the Shamen (Ebeneezer Goode) as having pro-drug lyrics. It was argued that these songs should be banned as their lyrics were leading young children into harmful illegal drug use.

## Aims and Objectives

This paper aims to test whether there is any evidence to support claims that today's adolescents are copying the drug using behaviours of their pop idols. This was done using data collected from three samples of Scottish schoolchildren (N = 3928) collected between March 1996 and April 1997. Respondents' favourite types of music were compared with whether they had lifetime experience of illegal drug use.

In earlier research conducted by these authors, fans of rave music were more likely to have used both legal and illegal drugs than were other adolescents (Forsyth et al, 1997). This paper aims to confirm whether this relationship has survived into the late 1990s. Such a finding would indicate that one of the strongest associations between music and drugs is that of a style (rave) which has no pop stars to emulate and few lyrics to think about. However, this paper also aims to focus on other styles of music. In particular it is intended to examine whether any relationships exist between individual music acts, who might behave as role models, and illegal drug use by their school age fans. In this way the hypothesis that young fans of pop stars with a drugs record are likely to become illegal drug users can be tested.

## Method

### Sampling procedure

Data were collected from three samples of Scottish school children. The first of these, sample A, comprised 620 pupils attending three independent schools in Scotland. These data were collected in the spring of 1996. Sample A comprised the equivalent of two classes from each school year between secondary years one (S1, aged 11–12 years) and five (S5, aged 15–16). Two of the three schools also provided the equivalent of two classes from secondary year six (S6, aged 17–18). This sample had a mean age of 14.7 years (range 11 to 19). Two of these schools only accepted female pupils, the other was co-educational, resulting in Sample A having a female majority (81.3%).

Sample B consisted of 1990 children attending twelve comprehensive (state) schools in the west of Scotland. Sample B data was collected in the spring of 1997. This sample was recruited by randomly taking two mixed ability classes from each of the four compulsory school years S1 to S4 from each participating school. It was not possible to recruit any older respondents (S5 or S6 pupils) from these state schools as many (in some cases most) children leave at the end of S4. The mean age of this sample was 13.4 years (range 11 to 16) and almost half were female (49.4%).

Sample C consisted of 1318 children who were recruited from five state schools in the city of Dundee, in the east of Scotland, during the same time period as Sample B was collected. Sample C comprised all S3 and S4 pupils who were present in each school on the day that the researchers called. It should be noted that approximately one fifth of this sample were previously interviewed, as S1 or S2 pupils, in November 1994 (see Barnard et al, 1996) during an earlier survey sweep of that city conducted by the authors. Sample C had a mean age of 14.5 years (range 13 to 16, though 95.8% were aged 14 or 15) and was 49.8% female.

An identical structured questionnaire was administered to each of these three samples of schoolchildren. This was done in examination conditions, where pupils' desks were separated to discourage conversation. Questionnaires were completed in the absence of teachers. On the first page of the questionnaire the children were asked the open-ended question, "What is your favourite kind of music?". Later questions asked whether the children had ever used any illicit drugs. In this paper simple comparisons were made between children's choices of music and whether or not they had ever used any illicit drug.

### Defining Musical Style

This method is the same as was employed in previous surveys of schoolchildren in Dundee city in 1994 and Perth and Kinross District in 1996, which tested the relationship between music and drug use then (see Forsyth et al, 1997). As might be expected a very large number of different responses were given to the music question. These included many different musical styles, genres of musical styles and musical acts. Other answers included the citing of a musical instrument, a youth culture or a tradition, liking all music, liking no music and stating what music they did not like (rather than citing a favourite). It should be noted that some respondents gave more than one answer. Often this involved the citing of examples, such as "Soul e.g. Toni Braxton".

From these answers it was possible to categorise groups of answers by musical style. The most obvious and simplest classification is that of rave and non-rave styles (see Forsyth et al, 1997). Rave music was defined as being distinct from all other music in that it involves electronic (computer) music rather than that produced by instruments and that vocals (if any) are sampled (on computer or mixing desk) rather than being songs. Likewise, rave acts tend to be either DJs (e.g. Tom Wilson) or dance events (e.g. reZ) rather than live musicians, vocalists or bands. These objective differences make rave styles and acts easier to define than any non-rave styles. For example, how legitimate would it be to include heavy metal, grunge and punk as genres of rock and roll. Decisions about which (if any or how many) of these categories acts such as Nirvana or Guns and Roses could be included necessarily involves a degree of subjectivity.

Another way in which rave music differs from most other styles is in chronology. Rave (acts and genres) originated in the dance drug scene that began in the late 1980s (Smith, 1995; Collin, 1997). Acts popular before 1988 could not be regarded as rave acts. This chronological method can also be used to classify other music styles and acts. For example acts who became famous before or after a certain point in history, such as 1960s acts (e.g. the Beatles).

## Results

Samples A, B and C were combined to give a total sample size of 3928 respondents. The mean age of the total sample was 13.9 years (range 11 to 19, though 92.7% were in the range 12 to 15 years). A small majority (54.1%) was female. There were 182 different responses to the music question (excluding those who only stated what music they did not like). This was composed of 76 musical styles and 106 musical acts. These musical styles and acts are shown in order of popularity in Table 1. In this table the number of respondents who cited each particular style or act is indicated. The number of these respondents who had ever used an illegal drug is shown by the figure following in brackets. For example the most popular answer given was "pop" which was the favourite of 1147 school pupils, 206 (18.0%) of whom had used an illegal drug.

### Rave Style and Drug Use

In Table 1 musical styles and acts which can be defined as rave styles are indicated in bold italics. The generic term rave was the answer given by the most drug users of all the styles and acts cited. It can be seen that there was a tendency for all rave styles and acts to have relatively high proportions of drug users among their fans. Indeed if all these styles and acts are summed together, a majority (56.5%) of citations of this form of music were made by drug users. This confirms previous findings by Forsyth et al (1997) which found that fans of rave music were more likely to be users of both illicit and licit drugs than other children of this age group who were not fans of this form of music.

### Sub-Genres of Musical Styles and Drug Use

When the types of rave music styles listed in Table 1 were compared, a pattern between the generic term "rave" and the style's sub-genres was revealed. Taking responses with ten or more citations, all rave genres (techno, hardcore, house, jungle, club and bouncy) had a substantial majority of fans who were drug users (see Saunders, 1995 or Smith, 1995, for definitions of these genres). The only rave act to be cited by more than ten respondents (reZ) also had a large majority of fans who were drug users. However, respondents who cited the generic term "rave", rather than a more specific rave genre or act, were proportionally less likely to be drug users. In fact a small majority of these children were non-users (52.7%). This would seem to indicate an increasing level of sophistication in musical choice among drug using teenagers. This pattern may also apply to other non-rave music styles. For example a minority of rap music fans were drug users. However, respondents who cited rap acts (Cypress Hill, Ice Cube, Snoop Dogg, 2Pac and Wu Tang Clan), though individually small in number (*n* = 10), were invariably drug users.

### Non-Rave Musical Styles and Drug Use

It is clear from Table 1 that several music styles other than rave appear to be identified with by illegal drug users in this age group. Again taking only styles with ten or more citations, non-rave styles which had at least half their fans claiming to be drug users were "alternative", Irish sectarian, oldies (dating from the 1950s to 1980s), reggae, hip hop, soppy love songs and punk.

It is noteworthy that, from all answers given with ten or more citations, no musical style (or act) could be described as having only drug-free fans. That said some styles had a relatively small number of fans who had ever tried illegal drugs. Notable among these, with fewer than one-fifth of their fans being drug users, were pop, classical, country, modern 1990s music (in contrast to the 1960s to 1980s oldies above) and traditional Scottish music (in contrast to the Irish music above). It is particularly interesting that respondents who stated they did not like any kind of music (*n* = 35) were among the least likely schoolchildren to have tried drugs (17.1%). Answers such as "I don't like music" and the non-specific "pop" would appear to represent the opposite extreme from rave genres in the musical sophistication and teenage drug use continuum.

### Musical Acts that might promote drug use

From Table 1 it can be seen that only three non-rave acts (The Beatles, Cast and Pink Floyd), with ten or more citations, had a majority of fans who were drug users. Two of these acts, The Beatles and Pink Floyd, would appear to fit into the "oldies" category of musical style mentioned above. In fact a large number of the acts listed in Table 1 with fewer than ten fans could also fit this description. Acts popular between the late 1960s and mid 1970s in this table include Bob Marley, The Doors, Bob Dylan, P-funk, Cat Stevens, Jimi Hendrix, The Kinks, Lou Reed, Rolling Stones, The Who, and Frank Zappa. These acts, all popular before any of these respondents were born, were almost invariably cited by drug users (of all these only one Doors fan claimed not to have used drugs). Taken together with The Beatles, Pink Floyd, the oldies music style and other 1960s styles (mod, Mersey, Motown and retro), music from this era received a considerable number of citations (*n* = 94). This music bloc had proportionally more drug-using fans (78.7%) than rave music, any combination of rave genres or any other popular music style.

With 159 citations, the group Oasis was by far the most popular act listed in Table 1 (more citations than the three next most popular non-rave acts combined). For this reason, rather than any particular association with drug use (only 42.1% of their fans had ever used drugs), this was the act with the highest number of drug-users (*n* = 67). There were only three other (non-rave) acts with more citations than The Beatles. These were Boyzone, the Spice Girls and Blur. Like Oasis these are all 1990s acts, in the charts at the time of data collection. Also in common with Oasis, a majority of each of their fans had never used drugs.

## Discussion

Although the most important music style associated with drug use remains rave, the findings reported here indicate that this is a somewhat simplistic view. Sub-genres of rave music had proportionally more drug-using fans than was the case with the generic term.

Like the sub-genres of rave music, several other forms of music — reggae, punk, hip hop, Irish sectarian, love songs, oldies (1960s–1970s) and that simply described as "alternative" — also had a majority of fans who had used illegal drugs. At first glance these styles would seem to make up an unusual record collection. Perhaps a clue to their popularity among drug users lies in the number of persons citing them. All of these styles had a number of fans in the range 14 to 31 respondents. This is about one-tenth the number of fans of the most popular mainstream non-rave styles (other than pop) such as indie, dance music or rock and roll. This would appear to support the hypothesis of more sophisticated musical styles being associated with drug use as compared to the mainstream. This may account for the term "alternative" being associated with drug use. At least an increased tendency by drug users to reject the mainstream pop music of today is implied.

Why the above non-rave styles should be popular with the young drug users in the 1990s rather than music styles with past or present drug associations (e.g. jazz or rave) is unclear. Of these styles it may be argued that hip hop, reggae and punk have some drug associations. However, this could not be said about love songs or Irish sectarian music. This latter category is unusual here in that it is a composite of different answers, including "rebel songs", "the orange walk" and "Fenian". Unlike pop stars, Northern Irish politicians (on either side of the sectarian divide) have not been accused in the British media of making pro-drug statements. Perhaps the 1950s to 1980s oldies category is the most interesting of all. The acts cited by respondents that would fit into this category, such as The Beatles, Pink Floyd, Bob Marley and The Doors, certainly have both personal drug use associations and sang drug-related lyrics. When the media report drug use by these oldies this tends to be done in less condemning terms than when the same is reported about today's pop stars. For example, at the time of scandal surrounding the "ecstasy outburst" by East 17 star Brian Harvey (*Evening Times*, 1997), 1970s act David Bowie made similar statements about his use of drugs (*Daily Record*, 1997b). Bowie's comments were not so vociferously condemned by the press (if at all), presumably because acts like him did not (in the view of adults) appeal to teenage fans. Although none of these teenagers cited Bowie, many of his contemporaries were cited, and if so this was almost invariably by drug users. It may be surprising to many that, on the evidence of this research, acts such as The Beatles, Bob Marley and The Doors are more popular with today's teenagers than East 17. Although none of these respondents are old enough to remember when John Lennon, Bob Marley and Jim Morrison were alive, these artists' behaviours and lyrics would appear to have a greater influence over young people today than many of the acts being targeted by anti-drug campaigners. The acts singled out for their pro-drug lyrics on the *Newsnight* (1997) programme were not popular with this sample. Despite local Scottish connections, none of these children mentioned the Shamen and only eight cited Pulp (three of whom had used drugs). The evidence of this research indicates that many of today's pop music acts, such as Pulp and the Shamen, are being unfairly scrutinised. Of today's stars only Oasis, who had many more citations than anyone else, had more drug-using fans than The Beatles. Although acts from the 1960s and 1970s would appear to be proportionally more closely associated with drug use than any other category of music, it would be much more difficult to legislate against these acts than the likes of Pulp, The Shamen, Oasis and East 17. These acts are popular with teenagers, but also with adults (presumably including the parents of these respondents) and are now seen as important in British cultural history, as well as being of considerable economic importance. It is also of course easier to demonise and create moral panic around the new (Cohen, 1972) rather than the familiar, and easier to blame the living than the long since deceased.

There is little evidence here to support any calls for the censorship of music. Even had such a connection been found, the same difficulties in definition as was encountered in this paper would hamper any attempt at drug lyric classification. In any case such legislation would seem likely to fail if not prove counterproductive. Banned songs or acts will only have their kudos increased. As recently seen with the rave scene, when a music style is driven underground by successive legislation its perceived sophistication and attractiveness to young people can only be heightened.

## Conclusion

There was little evidence here to support the view that adolescents who follow a particular pop music act are likely to emulate their drug using example or be influenced by their pro-drug lyrics. This was particularly the case with the pop stars of today who have often been accused of having such an influence. However, there was some evidence to suggest that children who are fans of the popular music stars from past decades, such as the Beatles, were more likely to have tried illegal drugs. Whether this is because these oldies are more closely associated with drugs (through behaviour or lyrics), whether their music is more accessible to drug experienced adolescents (of whatever generation) or whether, like drugs, these acts have more sophisticated (adult) associations in the eyes of today's teenagers requires future research.
