*From David F. Musto, [The American Disease: Origins of Narcotic Control](/library/policy/the-american-disease/). Oxford University Press, 1973 (3rd ed. 1999). See also the preceding chapter: [2. Diplomats and Reformers](/library/policy/the-american-disease/diplomats-and-reformers).*

In January 1912 Wright returned to the United States with two goals: increasing the number of signatories to the Convention and dispelling any doubt that this nation would pass the necessary domestic legislation. Southern resistance to any invasion of states' rights made Democratic control of the House especially significant for the narcotic reformers. Representative Francis Burton Harrison, a well-born Tammany Democrat, agreed to shepherd the antinarcotic legislation through the House. Harrison's task was to assure his colleagues that the various trade interests and concerned parties had achieved a generally acceptable narcotic bill or at least one that would engender no unyielding hostility. He did not display as much interest in the specific form or philosophy of the legislation as in its political viability.

Strongly backed by such adamant reformers as Drs. Harvey Wiley, Alexander Lambert, and William Schieffelin, Dr. Wright believed that the legislative goal should be elimination of narcotics except for medical purposes. As a result, the first Harrison bill in the 62nd Congress did not differ greatly from the ill-fated Foster bill of 1910.[^1] It contained no provision for exempting small amounts of narcotics in patent medicines. Revenue stamps, record keeping and various details such as bonds, license fees, and severe penalties were retained, although still strongly opposed by the retail druggists. Wright could not easily bring himself to compromise. Weakening the bill would have made him susceptible to the same criticisms he heaped on the foreign governments that did not rigorously and heroically stamp out the evil of narcotics.

## The National Drug Trade Conference

Believing that the movement to control narcotics was gaining strength and that some legislation was likely to be enacted, the American Pharmaceutical Association sought to give the various components of the drug trades a united voice, or at least a forum, in which the interests of each would receive the maximum possible support of the others. Meeting in Denver during October 1912, the association called for a convocation. As a result, the National Drug Trade Conference (NDTC) was created and met in Washington, D.C., on 15 January 1913, with its chief business the proposed antinarcotic bill. Each major trade association was permitted three representatives, and no resolution could be passed unless it was unanimous.[^2] This set of rules worked relatively well for the problem at hand because pressure from outside forces was considerable. During the three-day meeting, constituent members were able to compromise on a common position. In later years, however, and on other subjects, the NDTC reached unanimity less often.

All groups opposed the Harrison bill as it then stood. The American Association of Pharmaceutical Chemists and the National Association of Medicinal Products (NAMP), both makers of prescription drugs, were against the legislation on grounds of its "too complex procedure." The National Association of Retail Druggists (NARD) found inconsistencies, especially in the keeping of records. The National Wholesale Druggists' Association would abide by the law if enacted but also found it inconsistent in various respects. The principle of the law was endorsed by the APhA although the details appeared to be a burden on the retail drug trade, especially the record-keeping provisions. In a familiar jousting for superiority, Wright invited the NDTC to meet with him at the State Department, but the NDTC, as its first unanimous resolution, responded by inviting Wright to meet with them at the Hotel Willard.

He accepted, and Wright and Representative Harrison heard the druggists' criticism. The NDTC members found that evening session an irritating encounter with the federal bureaucracy for they had worked at great length to prepare a detailed and unanimously approved criticism of the bill. For Representative Harrison, who had to meet his wife at the theater, "or she would fear I had taken an overdose of narcotics," it was a rushed evening. Wright got so angry with the proposed changes to his bill that he walked out of the meeting declaring that any further discussion would take place at the State Department. This hectic encounter strengthened the NDTC's resolve to approve no bill until its views were taken into consideration. Wright's angry action had helped unify opposition to the Harrison bill.[^3]

A few days after the confrontation, Harrison decided that Wright would have to work out a bill suitable to the NDTC; he would then see to its passage through the House. Several revisions suggested by the NDTC were accepted into the bill.[^4]

The NDTC established an Executive Committee based primarily in Washington and able to meet quickly to discuss the sometimes rapidly changing status of legislation.[^5] The American Medical Association, which also had been consulted about the Foster bill, was always represented in the NDTC conferences, either formally or informally.

The NDTC remained active throughout the legislative history of the Harrison Act and also served as a lobbying organization for the drug trades in subsequent narcotic legislation. In 1921 the NDTC was instrumental in getting a rider attached to the Harrison Act that specifically exempted various articles, such as liniments, from the Act's restrictions. In that same year a cough-syrup preparation containing heroin was also exempted.[^6]

## The AMA and Narcotic Legislation

The American Medical Association's interest in narcotic legislation antedated the Harrison bill. In 1910 the AMA had established a Committee on the Acquirement of the Drug Habit, a title implying that the AMA's concern was with the addiction problem rather than economic or legal interests.[^7] Like other groups, the AMA opposed the Harrison bill as it stood in 1912, but the AMA's objections were less concerned with pharmacists' problems of record keeping than with physicians' problems of maintaining privacy and freedom from interference in their practice. Dr. Alexander Lambert, a professor at Cornell and personal physician to President Theodore Roosevelt, took a leading role in representing the AMA's views. In the next decade Lambert would be a key figure in the New York City Mayor's Committee on Drug Addiction (1918–19) and in attempts to create and maintain narcotics clinics and morphine maintenance programs in the early 1920s.

Lambert met with Wright and Harrison in January 1913 and described the AMA's opposition.[^8] Like other organizations, the AMA wanted physicians to be exempt from the taxation scheme, for the requirement of paying a fee in order to prescribe a drug was felt to compromise medical practice. The AMA also opposed the general provision requiring anyone dispensing a narcotic to keep a record of the transaction. The argument was that physicians could not ethically keep a patient's name on the business records required by the Harrison bill; patients would rightfully object to having their addiction or illness on public record.

Wright insisted that recording patients' names was essential; otherwise, addicts would go from one physician to another and build up unrestricted supplies of narcotics. A physician who truly cared about his patients would not, Wright maintained, object to keeping their names on record. Wright and Lambert came to an agreement of sorts: the physician would be required to keep records, but those records need not be open to public inspection without a court order. This provision was incorporated in later versions of the Harrison bill.[^9]

After the initial conferences with Wright, the AMA asked that further discussion on the Harrison bill be held with its representatives rather than with the NDTC's representative for medicine.[^10] The AMA feared that its positions were being confused with those of the NDTC, whose interests were seen as primarily commercial. The AMA wished to be seen as primarily concerned with public health rather than economics.

Although the AMA's Committee on the Acquirement of the Drug Habit took a temperate and measured position on narcotic legislation, other voices in the medical profession were less moderate. Some physicians held the view that drug addiction was a disease requiring medical treatment and that a punitive law would prevent proper treatment of addicts. They agreed that physicians should be free to prescribe narcotics to addicts as maintenance therapy.[^11]

## The Pharmacists and Narcotic Legislation

Among the most persistent opponents of the Harrison bill were the retail pharmacists, organized chiefly in the National Association of Retail Druggists. Pharmacists would bear the heaviest burden of any drug trade group in complying with the proposed law, especially the record-keeping provisions. They were required to register, pay a small fee, and keep elaborate records of all narcotic transactions. The pharmacist had to keep a bound register in which he recorded each narcotic purchase and sale, and these records were subject to inspection by federal agents.

The retail druggists argued that their trade in patent medicines containing narcotics was already licensed and taxed under existing state laws and that additional federal requirements created an unnecessary burden. They also pointed out that if the Harrison Act required detailed records for all narcotic transactions, the cost of compliance would be prohibitive for small pharmacies.[^12]

A joint committee set up by the State and Treasury Departments attempted to write a bill acceptable to the drug trades, the medical profession, and the Internal Revenue Bureau, which would have enforcement responsibility. Wright felt he was close to the end of a three-year fight with trade interests. At last, on 10 June 1913, the chairman of the NDTC wrote Wright that the drug trades accepted the Harrison bill as revised.[^13]

The compromise Harrison bill exempted patent medicines containing small amounts of narcotics (less than 2 grains of opium, or less than 1/4 grain of morphine, heroin, or cocaine per ounce or per dose) from the Act's main provisions.[^14] These preparations did not require a prescription or a physician's registration. Manufacturers and wholesalers also had less onerous requirements than retail pharmacists. These exemptions significantly limited the Harrison Act's reach, for many popular remedies fell below the threshold quantities specified.

## Passage of the Harrison Act

The Harrison Act was introduced in the 63rd Congress as H.R. 6282. It passed the House on 26 June 1913, but the Senate declined to act on it during that session.[^15] The Senate finally passed the bill with amendments on 9 December 1914; the House concurred the same day. President Woodrow Wilson signed the Harrison Narcotics Tax Act into law on 17 December 1914. It went into effect on 1 March 1915.

The Act required all those who produced, imported, manufactured, compounded, dealt in, dispensed, sold, distributed, or gave away opium or coca leaves and their derivatives to register with the district office of the Internal Revenue Service and to pay a graduated occupational tax. Unregistered persons could obtain narcotics only on a physician's prescription. The Act was administered by the Treasury Department rather than the Public Health Service, signaling from the outset that the dominant federal conception of addiction was as a problem of law enforcement rather than medicine.[^16]

The mechanics of the law required that every physician who prescribed narcotics must register with the IRS and pay a dollar fee. Prescriptions had to be on official forms and written in good faith to a patient in the course of professional practice. What "the course of professional practice" meant was not defined in the Act, and this ambiguity would bedevil the law's enforcement for decades. Could a physician prescribe morphine to a known addict as maintenance therapy? The Act's language seemed to permit it; later Treasury interpretations and court decisions would say it did not.

The Harrison Act was the product of compromise. The drug trades had successfully lobbied for the patent-medicine exemption, the limited record-keeping burden for some sectors, and the modest tax rather than outright prohibition. The reform constituency had achieved federal supervision of the narcotic trade, a national registry of narcotic dealers, and prescription requirements that limited lay access to opiates and cocaine. Neither side felt fully satisfied.[^17]

The Act's enforcement was taken over by the Narcotics Division of the IRS, which gradually expanded its interpretation of "legitimate medical use." During the years 1919–24 a series of Supreme Court decisions — Webb v. United States (1919), Jin Fuey Moy v. United States (1920), and United States v. Behrman (1922) — severely restricted the ability of physicians to prescribe narcotics to addicts for maintenance purposes. The clinics that had opened in many cities to provide maintenance to addicts were forced to close. The American medical profession's brief experiment with treating addiction as a disease had effectively been terminated by the combination of Treasury enforcement and an unfavorable Supreme Court.[^18]

The consequences were significant. Addicts who had been receiving stable supplies of narcotics through legitimate channels were forced onto the black market. The illicit trade in heroin expanded rapidly during the 1920s. The price of narcotics rose dramatically. The addict population, which might have stabilized or declined under a maintenance system, was now associated with crime and the underworld in the public mind. The Harrison Act, intended as a regulatory measure, had inadvertently created the conditions for a large and persistent criminal trade in narcotics.[^19]

## Drugs, Alcohol, and the Public

The Harrison Act did not emerge in a vacuum. The years 1913–14 were ones of extraordinary agitation over the use of habit-forming substances, and narcotic reform was deeply intertwined with the campaign for national Prohibition. The same reform energies — evangelical Protestantism, progressive confidence in government action, fear of immigrant and racial minorities — animated both movements. The same politicians and publicists appeared in both campaigns.[^20]

One of the most colorful figures linking the two was Richmond Pearson Hobson, a naval hero of the Spanish-American War who had become famous for attempting to block Santiago harbor with the old collier *Merrimac*. Hobson's exploits had been celebrated in verse, and he had become a romantic celebrity; a confection was even named after him, "Hobson's kisses."[^21] He moved from his personal adventures to warning of the "yellow peril," particularly the Japanese. He served from 1906 to 1915 in Congress from his native Alabama, and became one of the leading Prohibitionists. Defeated in 1914 in a Senate race against the powerful Oscar W. Underwood,[^22] he still had time to introduce the Hobson Resolution on Prohibition which foreshadowed the eventual defeat of the wet forces. In 1922 he progressed from liquor prohibition to narcotic prohibition with the zeal, platform style, exaggerated fears, and pseudoscientific warnings which had marked his liquor speeches. In 1933 he published *Drug Addiction: A Malignant Racial Cancer*; the following year Congress approved his promotion on the retired list to rear admiral. He died in 1937.

The Harrison Act debate in the House illustrated both the connections and the tensions between narcotic and alcohol reform. Prohibitionists pointed out that the federal government had already taken steps against the narcotic evil and should not hesitate to take a similar stand against alcohol. But opponents of Prohibition also used the specter of narcotics to support their arguments: if narcotics were regulated rather than prohibited, why should alcohol be treated differently?[^23]

The public rhetoric surrounding both movements drew heavily on racial and immigrant stereotypes. In the case of narcotics, the Chinese and the opium den, the Negro and cocaine, the "dope fiend" of lurid newspaper accounts — all served to distance the problem from respectable white America and to justify punitive rather than therapeutic responses. The Harrison Act incorporated this ambiguity: it was framed as a revenue measure, and on its face it merely regulated rather than prohibited the narcotic trade, yet the administrative apparatus created to enforce it would use the Act as a tool of prohibition in everything but name.[^24]

The passage of the Harrison Act in 1914 was a beginning, not an end. The subsequent decades would see the Act's reach extended by administrative decision and judicial interpretation far beyond what its sponsors had intended or the drug trades had accepted. The fundamental question left unresolved by the Act — whether addiction was a medical problem or a criminal one — would remain at the center of American drug policy for the rest of the twentieth century.[^25]
