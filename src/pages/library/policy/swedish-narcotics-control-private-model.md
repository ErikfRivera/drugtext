*Ted Goldberg, Department of Social Work, Stockholm University, Sweden*

*In Sweden the 'Swedish model' is thought to be the most successful narcotics policy in the world. In this article arguments in support of the model are critically examined. The number of hard-core misusers has increased at approximately the same rate after the Swedish model was instituted in the 1980s, as during the 'drug liberal' 1970s. Furthermore in the 1990s the Swedish model has been changed and it is now difficult to distinguish it from the policies many industrialised countries followed in the 1980s. As Swedish unemployment rates are presently on about the same level as those found in many parts of Europe in the 1980s, Sweden can expect a rapid increase in the number of hardcore misusers. An increasing number of Swedes will then question narcotics policies. As Swedes have been lead to believe that the only alternative to the Swedish model is legalisation, the number of such proposals will increase. Neither the Swedish model nor legalisation are considered effective in the Swedish setting, and a call is made for the development of alternatives. This requires changes in the theoretical understanding of the problem in Sweden. Suggestions are made as to elements of our current knowledge which should be incorporated into future narcotics policy.*

## Introduction

In Sweden the 'Swedish model' is generally accepted as the most successful program for combating the use of narcotics in the Western world. In this article I shall critically examine the arguments advanced by its proponents.

Current Swedish drug policy was instituted in the early 1980s. Its primary goal is 'a narcotics-free Sweden', and the principal means for achieving this are:

- Legal restrictions on production, sale, and use
- Protecting the country's borders against smuggling
- Prevention (primarily information campaigns)
- Treatment programmes
- Intervention on the street level

Upon deeper analysis these measures give rise to a number of questions, which will be the initial subject of my inquiry. Thereafter I will address some of the possible implications of the arguments presented for future drug control policy.

## Is 'A Narcotics-Free Sweden' a Feasible Goal?

Is it reasonable to have 'a narcotics-free Sweden' as the primary goal for Swedish drug policy? The answer can be yes if one believes that a country must have a lodestar and that by shooting for the heavens the chances of at least reaching the tree tops are enhanced. However, if the goal is to be taken literally, there are insurmountable problems. The practicality of drastically reducing the supply of narcotics in a country is limited by its ability to both intercept drugs brought to its borders, and prevent domestic production. I shall briefly examine the first of these tasks.

Not even the customs authorities themselves claim that they seize more than a small fraction of the narcotics brought to Sweden; 5% is a common estimate (see for instance Rosenkvist, 1992). The ingenuity and sophistication of the methods of smuggling is impressive. To take just one example: in 1983 one and a half metric tons of hashish were found on the beaches north of Gothenburg. The drugs were packaged in plastic bags and had either been anchored on the ocean floor in international waters and inadvertently cut adrift, or deposited on an island off the Swedish or Danish coast. Presumably the drugs were to have been smuggled into Sweden in pleasure craft. Is it reasonable to believe that it is possible to guard Sweden's almost 3000 km (2000 mile) long coast, with all its small islands and inlets?

The problem is magnified by the fact that the authorities are not simply dealing with 'normal' criminals. Governments, highly placed military officers, terrorist/liberation organisations and secret service agencies are all known to be deeply involved in the production and distribution of narcotics. (See for instance Goldberg, 1993, chapter 11; Ehrenfeld, 1990; McCoy, 1972). As long as revenue from narcotics is an important source of income, there is little reason to believe that the involved organisations will discontinue these activities.

Narcotics have been readily available in almost all of Sweden for many years. Anybody willing to exert themselves can buy illegal drugs. Not only have the authorities been unable to stop the country's traditional suppliers, there are also new ones establishing themselves in the business. Several of the Eastern European countries are experiencing serious economic problems, and since the dissolution of the Soviet Union, crime syndicates in Eastern Europe have become increasingly organised and export oriented. Narcotics, illegal alcohol and anabolic steroids from Eastern Europe are currently being sold on the Swedish black market. There is good reason to believe that this traffic will increase in the future (see Brink, 1993, p. A6).

Even before Sweden became a member of the EU the authorities were unable to control the flow of narcotics coming into the country. In the long term it is reasonable to expect that drug liberalisation elsewhere in Europe coupled to a 'Europe without borders', will further increase the availability of narcotics. Slowly but surely the goal of a narcotics free Sweden will seem all the more remote and meaningless to an increasing portion of the population.

## Is There a Swedish Model?

Upon closer inspection the components of the so called Swedish model do not differ greatly from those that constituted the narcotics policies of many other countries in the 1980s. Although details vary, as do the amount of resources appropriated to each component, Sweden is certainly not unique in its basic thinking about how to deal with the consumption of narcotics and the supply channels that make it possible.

Allocation of resources to different segments of a country's drug policy can vary over time. In Sweden during the 1980s, relatively large sums of money were earmarked for treatment programs. At the same time the country continued the 1970s policy of expanding legal restrictions. The Swedish model it was argued, counterbalanced repressive legal restrictions with humane treatment policies.

In the 1990s this balance has been changed. On the one hand Sweden continues to expand legal restrictions. For instance it has recently been made more difficult for prisoners (most of whom are drug offenders) to get a parole. The Conservative led coalition of 1991–1994 reduced government spending in almost all areas but wanted to increase the number of prisons. The current Social Democratic government has not changed the direction of the policies outlined by its predecessor. At the same time Sweden has drastically reduced funding to treatment facilities, and many of them have been closed. The Swedish model of the 1990s has increasingly come to rely on restrictive (and repressive) measures, and the role of treatment has been greatly reduced. Swedish drug policy has therefore all the more come to resemble the drug policies practised in other Western countries in the 1980s; the same countries for whom Sweden now claims to be a model.

But even if attention is focused only on the Swedish policies of the 1980s, where the fear of HIV/AIDS led to a relatively large allocation of resources to treatment facilities, research results do not support the claim that the Swedish model was successful. The only nationwide attempts to estimate the number of daily consumers of narcotics are the two studies published by a research team known under the abbreviation 'UNO'. The first is from 1979, that is shortly before we started to implement the Swedish model. This research was followed by a similar study in 1992. Using casefinding and capture-recapture methodology UNO found 12,000 'hard-core misusers' in 1979 and 17,000 in 1992; an increase of 40% in 13 years. (Olsson et al., 1993, pp. 30, 36, 47). However, since narcotics statistics can be interpreted in many ways this figure will be subjected to deeper analysis.

## The Number of 'Hard-Core Misusers'

Has the Swedish model led to a decrease in the number of 'hard-core misusers' in the country?

It is very difficult to give an unequivocal answer to this question. One problem is that all statistical data on narcotics consumption are tarnished by such factors as sampling problems, loss of data, problems in defining and operationalising concepts, ability/willingness to give correct answers to questions, lapses of memory, and changes in legislation and enforcement. This means that any and all published statistics might well be inaccurate, and comparisons between different years, different studies and different countries are problematic. Depending upon which data you put your confidence in, you can come to different conclusions. A further complication, which can't be considered here, is that one can never be certain as to what the picture would have looked like if the measures taken had never been implemented. In spite of these difficulties, there is no better alternative than to use the available data.

Before I continue I must clarify some concepts. In my own writings I distinguish between 'daily consumption of narcotics', defined as taking psychoactive substances as a recurrent part of everyday life, and 'recreational consumption', where an individual uses narcotics only on special occasions (see Goldberg, 1993, pp. 43–46). UNO uses the concept 'tung missbrukare' which I have translated as 'hard-core misuser'. In UNO's terminology a hard-core misuser is a person who either (1) injects narcotics or (2) uses them on a daily or almost daily basis regardless of the means of ingestion. In this paper daily consumer and hard-core misuser will be used as synonyms.

**Some pitfalls**

Let us now take a look at a common way proponents of the Swedish model present their arguments. Jakob Lindberg, currently second in command at the National Institute of Public Health and one of the founders of the Swedish model writes: 'We have good reason to believe that our data on the number of hard-core misusers in Sweden are uncertain' (Lindberg, 1992, p. 37). He then goes on to tell us that: 'it [is] my firm belief, based on all I know about our lack of knowledge in this area that we can't compare drug misuse in different countries other than in very general terms' (Lindberg, 1992, p. 38). Yet he draws the conclusion that 'our prevention strategy has been comparatively successful. Compared to other countries experimental misuse among our youth has been reduced to a very low level (3–4 per cent of today's 16-year-olds *admit* that they have tried narcotics as compared to 14 per cent in the 1970s). Intravenous misuse appears to be relatively stable and recruitment of youths to such misuse seems to have been reduced' (Lindberg, 1992, p. 39) (my italics).

I shall briefly discuss three major pitfalls in Lindberg's argumentation. First, based on the difficulty in collecting data on narcotics, Lindberg rejects data which does not support his opinion, while the validity of data which can be interpreted as positive to the Swedish model is not questioned. In particular, he warns us that other countries' drug statistics are not to be relied upon, but accepts without question information given by Swedish school children in a questionnaire filled in while they are in school (and possibly feel they are being observed by their teachers).

Second, Lindberg shifts back and forth between two different concepts: 'hard-core misuse' and 'experimental (mis)use'. To my knowledge no researcher has presented evidence that the number of hard-core misusers in Sweden has decreased. Instead, Lindberg and other proponents of the Swedish model refer to data that indicate that experimental (mis)use has declined. The theoretical conceptualisation of narcotics upon which not consistently distinguishing between hard-core misuse and experimental (mis)use is based is that sooner or later anybody who continues to take drugs becomes dependent upon them (see for instance Bejerot, 1979, pp. 28–30). In other words there are only two paths for experimental (mis)users to follow:

1. discontinue all consumption
2. become dependent (i.e. a hard-core misuser)

Logically there is a third choice — continue as an 'experimental (mis)user' for as long as one wishes — but this alternative doesn't tally with the idea that everybody who continues to take narcotics becomes dependent and loses control over their behaviour. If in reality Sweden has drastically reduced the number of experimental (mis)users (as seems to be indicated by research done on school children and military conscripts), and if experimental (mis)use is the gateway to hard-core misuse, it is relevant to ask why the number of hard-core misusers has increased instead of declining? Naturally one could argue that hard-core misuse has its own epidemiology and is not created by experimental (mis)use, but if this is the case, why should a country expend so much time and energy, and accept all the negative effects of control policies, just to reduce the number of experimental (mis)users, who are not destined to become hard-core misusers?

Finally, although Lindberg states that Swedish narcotics statistics are uncertain, that other countries' data on narcotics consumption are even worse, and that comparisons cannot be made other than in very general terms (Bejerot, 1979, pp. 37–39); he makes international comparisons and categorically claims that Swedish drug policy is superior.

**On recruitment**

In reality there is only one empirically based argument in support of the idea that the Swedish model functions. Some research can be interpreted as implying that recruitment of adolescents to drug consumption has been radically reduced. I shall now examine more closely the three types of studies cited in support of this claim.

*Studies of school children and studies of military conscripts*

Both of these types of studies have been conducted annually in Sweden for many years. Their results show that compared with the 1970s the number of subjects who admitted to having tried narcotics in the late 1980s had been drastically reduced. For 16-year-olds, the highest proportion of pupils who acknowledged having tried illegal drugs was 15% in 1972, while the lowest was 3% in 1989 (see Alkohol- och narkotikautveckingen i Sverige, 1996, pp. 200–201). Among military conscripts the equivalent highs and lows were 19.2% in 1980 and 5.8% in 1992 (Alkohol- och narkotikautveckingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 210). The same report notes that these studies principally illuminate experimental (mis)use (Alkohol- och narkotikautveckingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 123). That is, the reductions shown reflect that fewer youths admit to having experimented with narcotics. If fewer adolescents experiment with illegal drugs it can give the impression that the number of 'misusers' had declined. However, the number of hard-core misusers cannot be ascertained from these studies.

Furthermore, statistics from the past few years show a clear change in the trend. In 1995 there were about twice as many 16-year-olds (6%) who admitted to having tried narcotics as there were in 1989 (Alkohol- och narkotikautveckingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 201), and twice as many conscripts (12%) as compared with 1992 (Alkohol- och narkotikautveckingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 210).

While the studies of 16-year-old school children and military conscripts do not illuminate hard-core misuse, the two UNO studies do. UNO-92 found that the total number of hard-core misusers had drastically increased, but the number in the age group under 24 had been greatly reduced between 1979 and 1992 (Skog, 1993, p. 48). This might imply that even if Sweden hasn't managed to help those who already are hardcore misusers it has at least managed to limit the recruitment of adolescents. Skog, in an appendix to UNO-92 (Skog, 1993, pp. 49–50), accepts that recruitment in the younger age group was reduced in the beginning and middle of the 1980s but he argues that this was due to the AIDS epidemic being given a lot of attention in the media, rather than being a result of Swedish drug policy.

There is also reason to question if there really has been a reduction in recruitment in the younger age group. UNO's data on this point is problematic. UNO found only 250 hard-core misusers in the entire country in the age group under 19 (Skog, 1993, p. 48). This figure is extremely low and can be compared with 585 persons 15–20 years of age who were legally penalised for narcotics offences that year (Alkohol- och narkotikautveckingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 245). While there is a theoretical possibility that those who were legally penalised for narcotics crimes were not themselves misusers, I would hypothesise that adolescents in Sweden who deal or smuggle narcotics do so to support their own consumption. This implies that the greater part of these 585 youths were hardcore misusers in the sense that UNO uses the term.

While it is true that these two studies are not directly comparable as the latter also includes 20-year-olds, it seems reasonable to conclude that UNO's figure is an underestimate.

*Proportions of adolescents legally penalised for narcotics offences*

Another argument presented in support of the Swedish model is based on statistics which indicate that the proportion of people under the age of 25 who were legally penalised for narcotics offences has been drastically reduced during the period 1975–1992 (Alkohol- och narkotikautvecklingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 125). Logically this means that the proportion of those 25 years and over has increased. The authors of Alkohol- och narkotikautvecklingen i Sverige suggest that if one uses the concept hard-core misusers in the sense 'those known to the judicial authorities' these statistics give possible support for the idea that recruitment of adolescents to hard-core misuse has been reduced (Alkohol- och narkotikautvecklingen i Sverige, 1996, pp. 123–124). Such is possibly the case, but there are other ways of interpreting this data. Since the number of hard-core misusers aged 25 and over increased every year during the period under study, it is logical to expect that their proportion of those who were legally penalised would increase.

A conceivable explanation of the development which has taken place is that Sweden had a rapid increase in the number of teenage daily consumers of narcotics in the late 1960s and early 1970s. These were principally recruited from marginalised parts of the population, who previously turned to criminality and/or alcohol misuse. As narcotics became more easily accessible in Sweden during this period an increasing number of these people began to consume illegal drugs (see Goldberg, 1993, p. 108).

The rapid increase in the number of hardcore misusers of narcotics in the late 1960s and early 1970s can be explained by the fact that very few of the 'at risk' population had as yet been recruited. After a number of years, when a significant proportion of the susceptible individuals from this population had already started taking drugs, future increases were primarily based upon youngsters reaching a suitable age. This led to a tangible decrease in the rate of recruitment during the rest of the 1970s and beginning of the 1980s.

The relatively high unemployment rate among teenagers in the 1990s, combined with difficult living conditions for many refugees, has most certainly contributed to an increase in drug consumption in Sweden in the mid-1990s. There are no national studies of 'hard-core misuse' in Sweden after 1992 but the number of adolescents who were legally penalised for narcotics offences rose from 585 in 1992 to 735 in 1994 (Alkohol- och narkotikautvecklingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 245). This 26% increase in two years probably reflects an increase in hard-core misuse although it may to some extent be the result of changes in police priorities.

To return to the increasing proportion of those who were legally penalised for narcotics offences, a possible explanation is that in spite of a certain number of misusers 'exiting the field' each year there are enough remaining so that the total number aged 25 and over has increased annually. Therefore it is logical to find a proportional increase as well.

A comparison of UNO's data from 1979 and 1992 (Skog, 1993, p. 48) on the percentage of hard-core misusers in the age groups under 25 and 25 and over, and the proportion of those who were legally penalised for narcotics offences in the same age group (Alkohol- och narkotikautvecklingen i Sverige, 1996, p. 245) is shown in Table 1.

From this table it can be ascertained that in 1979 the proportion of those under 25 years of age who were legally penalised for narcotics offences was 20% higher than one would expect based on their proportion of the total population of hard-core misusers, and in 1992 this number had been reduced to 13%. In 1992 both in per cent and in actual numbers fewer youths belong to this category. There are several possible explanations for this. There might have been fewer young hard-core misusers to arrest. Alternatively, if the earlier statistics consisted of not only hard-core misusers but also experimental (mis)users, and if the latter group was reduced, the decrease can be due to there being fewer experimental (mis)users to arrest.

Another explanation has to do with the generally negative attitude in Sweden towards narcotics coupled with the police strategy of the 1980s in which law enforcers made a concentrated effort to make life difficult for those who consume narcotics. One effect of this has been that adolescents no longer openly demonstrate that they take drugs as they did in the 1970s. If this is coupled to the fact that allocation of resources for drug reconnaissance varies greatly over time, it may have become increasingly difficult for the police to uncover young (mis)users.

As has already been noted, there were more hardcore misusers above the age of 25 in 1992 than there were in 1979. Hard-core misusers are recidivists and reasonably this should mean that they are well-known to the police. Therefore it should be comparatively easy to arrest them and get a conviction. Furthermore, strange as it might seem, many older hard-core misusers on occasion actively try to get themselves arrested (see Goldberg, 1993, pp. 208–214).

Weighing the different arguments thus far discussed, it is difficult to conclude that there is clear evidence that recruitment to 'hard-core misuse' among adolescents has decreased. I shall now present one more piece of evidence.

## Recruitment of Hardcore Misusers

UNO-92 came to the conclusion that the number of hard-core misusers in Sweden increased by about 40% between 1979 and 1992. If this figure comes close to being accurate and recruitment has not occurred among adolescents, some combination of the following explanations must account for the increase:

1. Those who were hard-core misusers in 1979 still belong to the population and appear in the 1992 study.
2. Older people are being recruited.
3. Many hard-core misusers who started with narcotics during their youth managed to avoid all the authorities until after their 25th birthday.

There is a degree of uncertainty as to how to weigh each of these factors, but let us take a closer look at them. The first explanation seems very unlikely because it is known that some hard-core misusers:

- die young, as a result of suicide and other self-destructive acts, overdoses, unhealthy living conditions, violence, etc.
- change to alcohol and/or other legal drugs
- successfully complete treatment programmes
- mature out of drugs
- find God
- leave the country
- for other reasons stop taking drugs; for instance, they find a partner who doesn't use narcotics

In other words, there is good reason to believe that many individuals from 1979 were not in the follow-up study. This is compatible with estimates from several countries which indicate that about 3% of the hard-core misusers stop taking illegal drugs each year; which in turn is also compatible with Skog's calculation that about 40% of those who were hard-core misusers in Sweden in 1979 had left the population by 1992 (Skog, 1993, p. 49).

Therefore recruitment must have taken place and/or large numbers of hitherto unknown daily consumers must have been uncovered. It seems unlikely that recruitment could have been limited principally to people over the age of 25. The general consensus among researchers is that future hard-core misusers start taking drugs as adolescents. If the agencies which reported to UNO have uncovered hard-core misusers after their 25th birthday it would be reasonable to assume that they started taking narcotics earlier but managed to avoid being registered for many years. However, in a small and highly organised country such as Sweden, this seems highly unlikely. My conclusion is that none of the three possible explanations given above appear to be plausible.

**An interpretation**

As can be discerned from the material cited in this article, the figures from different studies and sources make it possible to draw different conclusions. To summarise my interpretation: if Skog comes close to the truth when he estimates that 40% of the 12,000 hard-core misusers in the 1979 study had left the population by 1992, and if UNO-92's estimate of an increase of 5,000 hard-core misusers between the two studies is fairly accurate, Sweden has recruited approximately 10,000 new hard-core misusers in the 13-year period. Therefore there must have been a very significant recruitment of adolescents during the period 1979–1992. There is simply no other reasonable explanation.

This implies that there is no support in the two UNO studies for the claim that the Swedish model has been effective in combating hard-core misuse. Sweden appears to have recruited almost as many daily consumers of narcotics during the 13 years between 1979 and 1992 (approximately 10,000) as were found in the 1979 study (about 12,000).

Furthermore, in the 1990s Swedish narcotics policy has gone through so many changes (towards more repression and less treatment) that it is difficult to claim that the Swedish model of the 1980s is in effect in Sweden today. If there ever was a narcotics policy in Sweden which differed so significantly from the policies of other Western countries that it was worthy of the name 'the Swedish model', it has now become increasingly difficult to differentiate Swedish policy from the policies many European countries followed in the 1980s.

## Does Sweden Expect Too Much of Its Drug Control Policy?

Many people in Sweden place great faith in the efficacy of its drug control policy. For instance, author and drug debater Gunnar Ohrlander writes in regard to the Swedish model: 'while European countries and cities have capitulated and more or less openly advocate the legalisation of narcotics, Sweden has succeeded fairly well in keeping the level of misuse constant' (Ohrlander, 1993, p. 5). Even if we ignore that this claim, as we have seen above, is almost certainly incorrect, there are other factors besides drug policy which influence the number of hard-core misusers in a country. In Sweden during the 1980s, for instance, governmental policy in general was directed towards keeping unemployment at a very low level. This was in sharp contrast to many other European countries where unemployment rose to more than 10% of the labour force (in certain minority groups and regions considerably higher). In Great Britain, for instance, several researchers have contended that the labour market policies of the Thatcher government contributed to a drastic increase in the number of daily consumers of narcotics in that country (see for instance, Peck and Plant, 1986, pp. 929 ff; Pearson, 1987b, pp. 69 ff).

In the early 1990s the Conservative led coalition government in Sweden abandoned the goal of full employment which had long been a major tenet in Swedish social policy. Even though the Social Democrats regained power in 1994, the unemployment rates have not improved. During the 1990s Sweden has had unemployment figures paralleling those of Western Europe during the 1980s. There is no reason to believe that the drug consumption stemming from this can be reduced with the measures Gunnar Ohrlander regards as the active ingredients in Swedish drug policy, i.e. 'disturbing the drug trade and offering treatment' (Ohrlander, 1993, p. 5).

Problems arising from unemployment, adverse living conditions and lack of faith in the future cannot be solved with the Swedish model — or any other drug policy for that matter. What must be understood is that narcotics are not a problem in and of themselves. Patterns of drug consumption in a country are deeply rooted in both the nation's history and current social conditions. In a country such as Sweden where daily consumers of narcotics primarily are recruited from a relatively small number of marginalised people, there are no easy measures, no declarations of a 'war on drugs', no information campaigns and no therapeutic techniques which can lead to a rapid reduction of the number of daily consumers.

## Legalisation in Europe

Why are an increasing number of people elsewhere in Europe advocating legalisation in the 1990s?

Before attempting to answer this question I must emphasise that there is no single, unified legalisation movement elsewhere in Europe, or anywhere else for that matter. Among those whom Swedish commentators designate as representing 'the legalisation movement' are people with very different ideas, ideals and goals. In the Swedish debate those who wish to ease a restriction on a particular aspect of drug consumption under certain circumstances are placed in the same category as people who would sell all drugs freely to anybody, anywhere, anytime.

The discussion presented here is based on the assumption that demands for changes in drug laws, as with any other political ideas, do not originate in a vacuum. Rather they should be looked upon as the reaction of a number of people to their experiences and to what they perceive as concrete problems. Change in public opinion does not occur by itself. It mirrors the belief people have that real change has taken place (regardless of whether or not this in reality is the case). It is therefore necessary to try to analyse the changes that have taken place which have led an increasing number of people in many countries to be attracted to various legalisation proposals.

One important element is that current narcotics policies have come to be considered unsuccessful by many people. At least three factors have contributed to this development:

1. In spite of great political and economic effort, no nation in Western Europe has managed to significantly reduce the number of daily consumers of narcotics. On the contrary, increases have been noted in country after country, for example Germany (see Lenke and Olsson, 1992, p. 29) and Great Britain (Pearson, 1987a, p. 13).
2. In some cities the number of daily consumers, and their misery, has become evident to large parts of the population. In Zurich, for example, the great amount of attention paid to 'Platz Spitz' in the mass media has surely had this effect.
3. Drug control policies have political, economic and moral costs. As people increasingly become aware of these costs demands for proof of positive effects spiral. But no such proof is forthcoming.

It is therefore not difficult to understand why an increasing number of people have begun to question drug policies, but why do so many turn to some form of legalisation instead of other possible measures?

## Are There Only Two Choices?

Part of the reason so many people are advocating some form of legalisation is to be found in the polarisation which has marked the drug debate. The alternatives have been reduced to two: either you support the current drug policies or you're a 'drug liberal'. People have been led to believe that there are no other alternatives. In Sweden, for instance, one of the leading voices in the drug debate, Nils Bejerot, writes:

> After 12 years of debate there are still some points to be settled. How prevention and narcotics policy in general shall be drawn up (strict, restrictive and consistent or lax, liberal and inconsistent) … (Bejerot, 1979, p. 8).

That drug policy could be formulated from a wider range of alternatives appears to be out of the question.

When an increasing number of people feel that drug policies have failed, and at the same time have been led to believe that the only alternative is legalisation, what remains other than to be for legalisation?

Presumably 'either/or-thinking' on drug policy is based on the fear that any questioning of current policy will weaken resistance to narcotics and lead to more drug misuse. In Sweden, anyone who questions the Swedish model runs the risk of being the target of personal attack. Critics are threatened, scorned, risk losing their jobs, etc. Sometimes the attacks are extended to a critic's family. For instance, two leading voices in the narcotics debate had the following to say about critics of some of Sweden's treatment centres: a critical social worker was described as 'a deplorable wreck', and a journalist/researcher was characterised as being extremely cold and insensitive. Concerning a third critic readers are informed that she lives together with 'one of the left wing party's most drug and treatment liberal profiles' (Westerberg and Andersson, 1991, p. A4).

Perhaps to a non-Swedish audience these comments might appear more childish than abusive. But in Sweden language tends towards understatements, and in the context of Swedish culture these comments are very drastic and cutting. Such statements could possibly be excused if they were spoken 'in the heat of battle' and later mollified, but the authors of the article referred to above chose to publish it a second time about a year after it first appeared. Few people wish to expose themselves and their families to this kind of abuse. Many Swedes who have a different understanding of narcotics have permitted themselves to be silenced and thereby left the field open for either/or-thinking.

Defenders of the Swedish model have increasingly had the field to themselves. The number of people who were openly critical diminished and those who continued to speak out experienced increasing difficulty in making their voices heard. The relatively few who were not silenced were, in Thomas Mathiesen's words, 'defined out', that is, considered to belong to the fringe and therefore there is no reason to discuss what they have to say (see Mathiesen, 1980, chapters 5 and 6). As critical ideas have not been taken seriously, the only two alternatives remaining have been those defined by adherents of the Swedish model; i.e. either accept the country's current drug policy or legalise narcotics.

## Will Current Swedish Policy Lead to Capitulation?

What will happen when and if an increasing number of people in Sweden, following the trend elsewhere in Europe, begin to feel that current drug policy is failing? What choices will Swedes feel are available to them? I agree with those who argue that to legalise narcotics is to capitulate and I do not find legalisation relevant in the Swedish context. But what choice other than giving up remains when and if 'the only path' proves to be a dead end? Which alternatives to the current narcotics policy might Swedes follow, that today's advocates of legalisation elsewhere in Europe can't discern?

To avoid getting into a situation where legalisation is considered the only choice at their disposal, Swedes must abandon either/or thinking and start discussing other alternatives. To be able to do this at least three basic changes are necessary.

First, Swedes must take another look at theoretical fundamentals; i.e. examine the assumptions upon which narcotics policies are based and subject them to critical reassessment. Examples of such assumptions are:

1. Humans are not simply biological organisms, they are also social beings. Concepts such as 'dependence' exaggerate biochemical processes and have led to a model of understanding which underestimates the significance of the individual's life experiences.
2. Human beings are active 'subjects' who choose among alternatives and attempt to achieve goals with what they do. To a great extent people's aims are socially determined. Therefore it is necessary to discuss such questions as: what goals are people attempting to achieve through drug consumption, why do certain people have these goals and why don't they turn to alternative means to reach them.
3. People are not only subjects, but also to a certain degree 'objects' in relation to the social conditions which have influenced them throughout their lives. They are born into an era, a nation, a social class and a family and none of this is of their own making. How society is organised, and the country's politics in general play a central role both in determining the goals its citizens set for their lives, and for their opportunities to achieve them.

Second, Swedes must reconsider those parts of our knowledge of narcotics and the people who consume them which have been silenced or ridiculed in an attempt to maintain a united front in support of current policy. For example, a large number of studies during the past three decades have shown that those who become daily consumers of narcotics most often have had extremely unusual life experiences compared with the rest of the population: *before they start taking drugs.* Hard-core misusers are principally recruited among people with many of the following factors in their background (see for instance Goldberg, 1993, chapter 5; Solarz, 1990; Holmberg, 1981):

- poverty
- at least one parent with high alcohol consumption
- corporal punishment
- serious conflicts in the home
- not brought up by both biological parents
- sexual abuse
- overcrowded housing conditions
- a multi-problem family, discrimination

The daily consumer to be has also shown at least some of the following psychosomatic symptoms:

- depression
- severe headaches
- stomach problems
- difficulty sleeping
- inability to tolerate stress
- anguish
- nervousness
- excessive aggression

Their adolescence was characterised by:

- lack of initiative
- giving up in the face of difficulty
- poor performance in school
- high levels of tobacco consumption
- sniffing solvents
- often being drunk
- criminal behaviour
- being known to the police and/or the child welfare authorities
- feeling inferior
- having a negative self-image

Even if there are exceptions, it is primarily individuals raised in families lacking economic and/or psychological resources who are at risk of becoming daily consumers of narcotics. It is extremely important that this be taken into consideration if attempts to come to terms with the misuse of psychoactive drugs are to be successful.

And finally Swedes must open themselves to ideas from other countries; albeit these must be adapted to the Swedish context. As a concrete example: about five years after Dole and Nyswander presented the idea of methadone maintenance in the early 1960s it was introduced into Sweden. From the very start methadone maintenance was controversial in this country and has been the subject of many debates and modifications. I would dare to say that the debate has led to many improvements in these programs and helped to avoid some of the problems to be found in many of the other methadone maintenance programs. Swedish programmes are well run, have saved many lives and abated much suffering.

The lesson to be learned is that ideas discussed in other countries can neither be rejected out of hand nor accepted at face value. As different countries have different social, political, economic and historic contexts, measures cannot be mechanically transplanted from one nation to another. Some proposals, in modified form, can become valuable contributions to Sweden's drug policy. Others cannot be implemented at all. The point being that we cannot know which are which until we have had a thorough and unprejudiced discussion.

## Concluding Remark

An alternative drug policy cannot be created by an individual or a particular profession. To be able to reach a conclusion as to what should be done, and then mobilise the necessary political support, requires open discussion and the combined experiences of many people and professions. Before Sweden comes to the point where a great number of people become doubtful of its drug policy, a credible alternative to both the Swedish model and legalisation must be developed. To achieve this we must be ready to re-examine cherished beliefs, scrutinise different ways of analysing the problem, and contemplate the pros and cons of alternative solutions. This in turn requires a climate where ideas instead of individuals are put to question.
