*By Reto Scholl MD, Erlinsbach, Chief of Aarau Hospital. Translated from Drogenmagazine NR 1, February 1995, for the International Foundation on Drug Policy and Human Rights & The Lindesmith Center by Marianne Apostolides.*

For years, Sweden's drug policy has been regarded as an example of a successful drug prohibition system. But careful analysis of official government data does not support this image.

The current Swedish drug policy — officially described as restrictive — was developed from a study documenting the Swedish experiences of the Sixties. To get a pure view of the Swedish rationale for their current policy, one should analyze only official state documents and the statements of government spokespeople.

## The Experiences of the Sixties and Seventies

Swedish authorities liberalized drug policy in the 1960s because of the fervent spirit typical of the decade (1). From April 1965 to May 1967, health authorities allowed some doctors to prescribe opiates and stimulants to a limited number of addicts (2). Those who were not a part of the prescribing trial still could not obtain drugs legally. In the two years of the experiment to medically control the supply of drugs, just over 200 patients received prescriptions. Although ten doctors were initially involved in the trial, most had a small number of patients and left the program soon after they joined. The most active doctor, Dr. S.E. Ahnstrom, took care of 156 clients of the 200 clients and was responsible for most of the prescriptions. He prescribed about 600,000 doses (3.335 kilos) of opiates and up to 3.3 million doses (15.1 kilos) of stimulants. While the bulk of Ahnstrom's clientele was initially opiate consumers, stimulant consumers later predominated.

Some of Dr. Ahnstrom's clients wrote their own prescriptions which the doctor then signed. Some people — especially those who did not receive further care or control — illegally sold a portion of their prescribed drugs. By selling the substances illegally, these clients became secondary suppliers or "satellites." The activities of the "satellites" formally medicalized and deregulated the supply of opiates and stimulants to a chosen number of drug consumers, even though those consumers still lived in a prohibitionist system.

The legal prescription program was stopped abruptly in 1967 following the death of a seventeen year-old girl named Anna Anderson on April 11, 1967. Her death was due to an opiate overdose which was treated not by medical professionals, but rather by "satellites" who gave her amphetamines.

The prescribing experiment was scientifically analyzed, and the results were published as a study in 1975 (2). Although this study usually isn't cited, it is the basis for every claim that Sweden's prescribing program led to a "massive rise of the number of addicts" in the Sixties (4, 5).

The authors of the Swedish study used what they thought was the only reliable method to recognize intravenous drug consumers: police records of the proportion of people arrested with scars or other signs of repeated intravenous injections. With a few exceptions, all people arrested in the Stockholm region between April 1965 and June 1970 were checked for scars. Those who always showed marks of repeated injections were classified in the study as intravenous consumers. The study was therefore called the Injection Mark Study (IMS). Although the search for injection traces does not detect people who ingest or smoke drugs, the authors claimed the method was very reliable for discovering intravenous consumers, with a margin of error of only 1–2%.

The authors of the study relied solely on the data from the injection markings of arrestees. They rejected all further methods — questionnaires, case research, etc. — of identifying and quantifying drug consumers. They were skeptical of other methods because of perceived difficulties in getting reliable data on the amount of drugs consumed; ensuring the identity of consumers; preventing consumers from being covered multiple times; and including consumers who belong to "non response" groups.

The police data from June 1965 to June 1967 show a significant rise in the percentage of arrestees who were intravenous drug users in that period — 20.1% for men and 15.9% for women. The percentage rise flattened after drug prescribing was stopped. From the correlation of these data over time, the authors concluded that there was a causal connection between drug use and the prescription program. The conclusion — that the rise in the proportion of drug consumers was caused by the permissive drug policy — is clarifying, but leaves several questions unanswered.

Is a causal connection at the basis of the contemporaneous changes in legal prescription policy and drug consumption prevalence?

A proper control group could have avoided the ambiguity about the nature of the link between the legal prescription policy and drug consumption prevalence. In other words, it could have determined whether the link was causal or correlative.

Sometimes correlative links are improperly labelled causal. The following fictitious example of storks in Green Valley illustrates the misapplication of correlative versus causal links: imagine that during the last 50 years, both the number of storks in Green Valley and the number of children born in Green Valley per female inhabitant have declined. According to the faulty reasoning employed in the Swedish study, the correlation of these data over time would show that children are delivered by storks.

The control group should have corresponded to the study group (the people arrested and examined for scars) in all parameters (age, gender, social and external circumstances, etc.) except for the parameter being researched (drug consumption). A valid control group should have been drawn from an area outside Stockholm. Since doing so would have been quite difficult, the authors of the study did not bother. By not creating a control group, they have forever deprived us of the ability to clearly determine whether the policy of deregulated drug prescribing caused the rising number of intravenous drug consumers.

Without a control group, the authors of the Swedish study have no means of refuting a plausible objection to their conclusions: namely that the population of intravenous drug consumers increased not only in Stockholm but throughout Europe and North America from 1965 to 1967, independently of drug policy. Such an objection would indicate that the increased use in Stockholm was only the local manifestation of a general phenomenon of the time.

In short, the conclusion of the study — namely that deregulated drug prescribing caused the increase in the prevalence of intravenous drug consumers — remains scientifically just a suspicion based only on a correlation of data.

Is the test population of IMS (Injection Mark Study) representative of a "normal population"?

IMS relies exclusively on people who were arrested. Therefore, although the study reports hard data, these data are irrelevant in respect to the general population.

To determine whether the IMS population was representative of the general Swedish population, the Swedish government independently collected a different group of data from May to December 1967. The Case Finding Study (CFS) was regarded as representative of a "normal" population. However, the list of 19 groups from which the CFS data were collected throws doubt on this conclusion.

Fourteen of the nineteen groups were preselected: five of the groups were classified as part of the criminal sphere; five others contained socially marginalized groups, one of which by definition consisted of drug addicts; two contained people in Infection Hospitals where hepatitis cases were concentrated; one consisted of people in psychiatric institutions. The five groups that were not preselected contributed to only 8.6% of all the CFS cases. A study whose population pool is 90% preselected can never be representative of the actions of an unselected general population.

How did the proportion of drug consumers, as calculated in the IMS, develop in the long term?

From 1967 to 1972, the drug prevalence curve flattened out. Even though the curve began to even out as the repressive policy began, it did not fluctuate in conjunction with the fluctuations in the extent of repression. Then in 1972, the curve was punctured by one short, striking decrease which was attributed to the arrest of a wholesale drug dealer. From 1974 to the middle of 1976, the percentage of intravenous drug users increased substantially although the repressive policy had not been eased. This renewed increase — an increase that occurred under a repressive policy — was even steeper than the June 1965 to June 1967 increase that was allegedly due to the permissive drug policy.

The graph shows that whether the drug policy was liberal permissive, traditional restrictive, extra restrictive or, once again, traditional restrictive, it did not have the slightest provable effect on the percentage of intravenous drug users among arrested people in Stockholm.

In short, the Swedish experience of the Sixties and Seventies, as documented in the 1975 IMS (2) and the 1978 follow-up study, shows the following results:

1. According to the 1975 IMS analysis, the hard data collected from Stockholm's arrested population show not only that the permissive drug policy of 1965 to 1967 caused an increase in the prevalence of IV drug use, but also that the repressive policy instituted in 1967 stopped the increase. However, these conclusions cannot be valid since the data are not valid: the sample population is not representative of a general unselected population, and the absence of a control group calls into question the data's implications.
2. According to a 1978 follow-up analysis, the same hard data show that a repressive drug policy could not prevent a substantial increase in drug use prevalence from 1974 to 1976. This increase under repressive policies was even steeper than the increase from 1965 to 1967.
3. The Swedish data therefore do not give any ground for a causal link between the changes in drug consumption prevalence and the changes in drug policy.

## The Situation Concerning Drugs in Sweden after 1977

The current drug policy, officially described as restrictive (6), is based on two analyses of experiences from the Sixties and Seventies (6, 3, 1) — analyses which, as shown above, are thoroughly insufficient. The arrest of a large drug dealer is assumed to be the reason the proportion of drug consumers among the arrested population sank from 55% to 36% from 1972 to 1973. And the legal prescribing program is assumed to be the reason the proportion rose from 19% to 36% from 1965 to 1967 (6, 3, 1).

The data on the dimensions of drug abuse (6) as provided by the Swedish National Institute of Public Health are based on case finding studies and surveys. In the eyes of Swedish authorities, these data confirm that Swedish drug policy is successful. Sweden is by no means a "completely drug free society." But by international standards, drug use is limited in Sweden.

Swedish authorities maintain that surveys prove the success of their policy. They point to surveys showing that only 5% of the schoolgoing population in 1992 had ever tried drugs (13% had done so at the beginning of the Seventies); that only 1% had consumed drugs during the last month (5% had at the beginning of the Seventies); that only 24% of the pupils believed they knew where to obtain drugs (34% had in 1977); that only 20% were offered hashish (32% were in 1976); and that only 3% were offered amphetamines (7% were in 1976) (6).

The "heavy" drug abusers data were collected in a national survey in which the police, prisons, social welfare authorities and medical institutions reported the cases known to them. The number of "heavy" drug abusers was estimated at 10,000–14,000 in 1979, of which 7,500–10,000 were intravenous users, and 1,500–2,000 of those intravenous users were daily users. In 1992 the estimated number of heavy drug abusers jumped by 6,000, from 14,000 to 20,000 people. But the percentage who were under 25 years old decreased from 37% to 10% (6).

In short, the collected data after 1977 were viewed simultaneously as a sign of decreased drug use (because of the decrease in self-declared drug use, mainly use of cannabis) and as a sign of increased "heavy" drug use with the proportion of consumers under 25 years old decreasing.

Analysis of the period after 1977 is based on soft data (surveys and case studies) which are notoriously unreliable means of collecting data on drug consumers. In addition, the data that mainly concern cannabis consumption were collected very unsystematically during a period of 24 years. Therefore, these sporadic and contradictory data are not sufficient to answer the main question — does the degree of repression of drug policy influence the prevalence of drug consumption?

## Conclusion

Official sources in Sweden maintain that current Swedish policy is responsible for the country's low drug use. But the soft data available since 1977 can't substantiate that assertion. The official reports represent state expectations, not documented facts. Despite the Swedish government's rhetoric, there are no data upholding the contention that a permissive drug policy would increase the number of drug consumers. When looking at facts substantiated by hard data from 1965 to 1977, one finds that a drug policy's degree of repression has no effect on the proportion of drug consumers in a population. Therefore, since increased repression does not decrease the proportion of consumers, the logical conclusion is that decreased repression would not increase the proportion of consumers.
