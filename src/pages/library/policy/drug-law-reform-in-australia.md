*Paper presented to the 8th International Conference on Drug Policy Reform, Washington, DC, 16–19 November 1994.*

*By **Ron Owens, BA**, Executive Director, Australian Drug Law Reform Foundation.*

## Introduction

The purpose of this paper is to present an overview of changes in Australian Drugs Policy during the period 1986 to 1994, to report on a proposed heroin trial and to offer an analysis of usage patterns as a result of changes in Australian drugs law, particularly with regards to the personal use and possession of marijuana.

In this paper I will look first at changes to the marijuana use and possession laws in South Australia and the Australian Capital Territory, and the rationale behind those changes; I will examine proposals for change in the State of Queensland and report on recent proposals for change enunciated at the Federal level.

The paper then looks at the proposal to conduct a heroin trial in the Australian Capital Territory and reports on the current position with regards to that trial.

Finally the paper reports on prevalence rates of marijuana usage following changes to the law in South Australia with some anecdotal evidence relating to personal usage rates in the Australian Capital Territory.

Personal use and possession of marijuana in South Australia and the Australian Capital Territory:

## South Australia

The impetus for reform of the cannabis laws in South Australia came out of the recommendations contained in the 1979 report of the South Australian Royal Commission into the Non-Medical Use of Drugs. That Royal Commission recommended, inter alia, that minor cannabis consumption not be treated as a criminal offence. In making such a recommendation the Royal Commission was able to cite several overseas jurisdictions, including ten states in the USA, which had taken such a step with apparent success. ()

Following the recommendations of the Royal Commission there was much argument proffered in South Australia for and against changes in the cannabis laws, particularly with regards to the Government's proposal to introduce an expiation notice system to deal with minor offences. () Primarily, advocates of the new system argued that formal criminal proceedings were ineffectual and were wasteful of both police and court resources. Further, they argued, that if health concerns were to be the primary focus of drugs policy, then the community should move away from criminal sanctions which, rather than eliminating the 'drug problem', has in fact aggravated it. Advocates also pointed to the positive experience of jurisdictions within the US and to the Netherlands.

Those arguing against change expressed the view that any relaxing of the law was an indication of societal sanction of the offences, which in turn would lead to a rise in usage rates and public flouting of the law. They further argued that:

> "the strongest way of marking society's disapprobation is the use of the criminal law". ()

Opponents also advanced a number of practical reasons such as perceived difficulties for operational police and the identifying of offenders. ()

Following much protracted debate, the South Australian Parliament finally passed the Controlled Substance Amendment Act in 1986 which provided for the expiation of simple cannabis offences.

Although the South Australian approach cannot be described as 'decriminalisation' in the strictest sense (if indeed there is an accepted strict sense of that term) it does remove some of the criminal stigma attached to the personal use of cannabis.

The latest survey data, to which I will return later in this paper, concludes that there is no evidence of any statistically significant increase in rates of weekly use over time in South Australia compared with the rest of Australia. ()

## Australian Capital Territory

The law relating to the personal use and possession of marijuana was amended in the ACT in 1992 as a result of the recommendations arising out of the Select Committee on HIV, Illegal Drugs and Prostitution, in its report *Marijuana and Other Illegal Drugs*. () That committee sought to evaluate alternative policy proposals in the context of evidence suggesting a rise in usage rates which was not being combatted by rising enforcement practices.

The committee examined worldwide literature reviews on the researched health effects of marijuana and closely examined the disparate legal penalties operating within Australia.

With regard to the health consequences of using marijuana, the committee formed an opinion that current research had failed to demonstrate the premise that moderate or controlled use of cannabis is more, or at least as, harmful as the licit drugs alcohol and tobacco; from which the committee then concluded that the perceived health threat level associated with cannabis usage would well be within current socially accepted health threat levels, () as exemplified by the community's broad tolerance of alcohol and tobacco usage.

At the time of the committee's report it was a commonality of all Australian law that marijuana be regarded as a prohibited substance, although the force of that prohibition varied from jurisdiction to jurisdiction. Thus the laws with respect to marijuana in the several Australian jurisdictions fell along a continuum; at one end of the continuum was the expiation notice system of South Australia — a small fine and no criminal conviction — and at the other end the harsh imprisonment penalties of the Queensland law for possession of even small amounts of cannabis. ()

It was apparent, in the committee's opinion, that the laws in its own jurisdiction, the Australian Capital Territory, had proven ineffective in combatting the use of marijuana. The intention of the law to discourage the availability and the use of cannabis and to punish those who supply or use it had not been fulfilled. In fact, the application and interpretation of the law, both by the enforcing agencies, mainly the police, and by the courts drew a definite, though covert, distinction between 'personal use' and other uses; a distinction which was not expressed in the legislation.

The committee enunciated the view that if the law is not capable of fulfilling the role assigned to it and if the law makers, the law enforcers and those prosecuting the law seek to lessen the effect of the law in certain areas, then that law should be reformed. And the committee further believed that this state of affairs represented the social position in the Territory with regard to the Territory's marijuana laws and so concluded that law reform in this area, as it was in the area of prostitution — the subject of the committee's first interim report, () — was necessary. ()

The committee finally took the position that there is nothing intrinsic to marijuana, in either its health effects or the social implications of its use, that would justify its personal use being an offence at law and thus recommended to the Assembly that the possession, cultivation and use of cannabis for personal purposes not be an offence at law. ()

Changes to the marijuana laws, which were by no means as far reaching as those recommended by the committee, were introduced by the Drugs of Dependence (Amendment) Act 1992 which came into effect on September 18, 1992. The legislative amendments introduce an expiation notice scheme that applies to both adults and juveniles.

The police are empowered to serve an offence notice on someone they believe to have committed a simple cannabis offence. If the prescribed penalty of $100 is paid within 60 days then the offence is discharged and no conviction is recorded. The fine of $100 is the same as that previously applying under the Act on conviction for possession of small amounts of cannabis.

Under the amendments a simple cannabis offence is defined as:

- the cultivation of not more than five cannabis plants;
- possessing not more than 25 grams of cannabis.

## Proposals for Change

### Queensland

In Queensland the relationship between the law and the personal use of marijuana is currently being examined by the Queensland Parliament's Parliamentary Criminal Justice Committee. The primary function of that committee is to monitor and review the discharge of the functions of the Criminal Justice Commission and to report to the Parliament on any reports submitted by the Commission.

The Criminal Justice Commission issued a cannabis discussion paper in 1993 () with the purpose of:

- presenting information on the nature, supply and use of cannabis, current laws pertaining to its use or production, and the effectiveness, cost and nature of law enforcement; and
- seeking submissions from interested community groups and individuals on preferred legislative, enforcement and social responses to the issues of cannabis use and production.

Following the issue of the Discussion Paper the Commission held a public inquiry into cannabis and, in July 1994, reported to the Parliament on its findings and made a number of recommendations for changes in the law with regard to cannabis use. () The recommendations are an attempt to bring Queensland into the 20th century. The Commission recommended that simple possession offence penalties should be reduced to a maximum of six months imprisonment and simple cultivation offence penalties should be reduced to a maximum of two years imprisonment. However first offenders should not have a conviction recorded and a fine not exceeding $500 should be imposed and further the cannabis paraphernalia offence should be abolished. The Commission has also recommended to Parliament that the powers of the police to detain a person and require them to submit to an internal body cavity search should not apply to the investigation of simple offences. ()

At the time of preparation of this paper the recommendations of the Criminal Justice Commission were still being reviewed by the Parliamentary Criminal Justice Committee. It is not expected that the committee will report before March 1995.

### Federal Proposals

Proposals for change with regard to cannabis laws have also been made at the Federal level. The proposals are contained in the Report of the National Task Force on Cannabis () prepared for the Ministerial Council on Drug Strategy. The Ministerial Council is a peak government body comprising the Health and Justice Ministers from each of the State, Territory and Commonwealth Parliaments; and includes the Health Minister from New Zealand.

This Australian report is significant for two important reasons: (a) for its very wide ranging recommendations and (b) for the four definitive research papers which go with the report. ()

### Recommendations

The Task Force Report contains 27 recommendations but there are just three to which I would like to draw your attention.

1. **Recommendation 10** — that jurisdictions consider discontinuing the application of criminal penalties for the simple personal use or possession of cannabis, without compromising activities aimed at deterring cannabis use.

   This recommendation, in effect, calls for uniform cannabis laws throughout Australia which remove the criminal penalties associated with personal use. In support of this position the Task Force said, in part:

   > "The argument has been put forward in the Task Force's commissioned paper on legislative options that the personal and social harms associated with total prohibition systems, particularly relating to the involvement of cannabis users in the criminal justice system, outweigh the harms arising from moderate cannabis use." ()

   The Federal Minister for Justice, the Hon Duncan Kerr, is now on the public record as saying that Australia could have uniform national cannabis laws by April next year. ()

2. **Recommendation 21** — that there be an analysis of the cannabis expiation systems operating in South Australia and the ACT, including comparisons with legal approaches in other states, and comparative analysis of the economic and social costs associated with the various systems.

   The significance of this recommendation is the call for a comparative analysis of the economic and social costs not only of any system which might be proposed, but also of those systems currently in operation, including the policy of prohibition. In its report the Task Force said:

   > "It seems that the key rationale which has formed the basis for the introduction of civil penalty schemes to date has been the view that the social and monetary costs associated with the existing prohibition schemes ... were too high." ()

3. **Recommendation 27** — that there be controlled research into the efficacy of synthetic cannabinoid products as treatments for nausea associated with cancer chemotherapy, for HIV/AIDS related conditions (including HIV/AIDS-associated depression and weight loss), for glaucoma, and as anticonvulsant, antispasmodic and analgesic agents.

   In reaching the conclusions which enabled the Task Force to make this recommendation, the authors of the commissioned paper *The health and psychological consequences of cannabis use* argued:

   > In a rational world, clinical decisions about whether to use pure cannabinoid drugs should not be abrogated because crude forms of the drug may be abused by those who use it recreationally. As a community we do not allow this type of thinking to deny us the use of opiates for analgesia. ()

## The Reports

The four Research Papers commissioned for the Task Force are the completion of considerable academic work in reviewing the relevant scientific literature, and can serve worldwide as valuable resources for researchers, policy makers, and other people and organisations. The significance of such important research is best summed up by the Task Force itself:

> Government policy relating to the use of all drugs, including cannabis, must be based on the most up-to-date information available, and must be flexible enough to change in the light of changing scientific views, or professional and public opinion. Policy development must involve debate and consultation at a broad community level. ()

## ACT Heroin Trial

The first report of the Select Committee on HIV, Illegal Drugs and Prostitution () relating to drugs concerned the proposal to conduct a trial on the controlled availability of heroin in the ACT. The issue of such a trial was raised by a number of people with the committee, including Dr Alex Wodak of St Vincent's Hospital, Sydney, and the need for such a trial is also a fairly recurrent theme in the literature. The Select Committee, through its Chairman, Mr Michael Moore, approached the National Centre for Epidemiology and Population Health (NCEPH), at the Australian National University, about the possibility of conducting a trial of the controlled availability of opioids in Canberra under the strictest of academic discipline. The purpose of the trial would be:

> "...to discover whether or not a policy of controlled heroin availability could ameliorate the massive burden which illegal heroin use currently imposes on Australian and ACT societies." ()

NCEPH, in conjunction with the Australian Institute of Criminology (AIC), looked closely at the proposal and, after a three month exploration of legal, ethical, political, medical and logistical issues, recommended that the trial proceed in four very clearly defined stages. These stages are:

- Stage 1 — Exploration of the issues surrounding the principles of such a trial;
- Stage 2 — Feasibility Study — Investigation of the logistics of conducting the trial;
- Stage 3 — Pilot Program — Piloting the procedures for the trial; and
- Stage 4 — Trial — Full scale trial of the proposed approach to controlled availability.

Stage 1 was completed in July 1991 and, on the basis of the resultant findings NCEPH and the AIC recommended that the trial proceed to Stage 2. The results of the Stage 2 feasibility study are expected to be reported in April 1995.

## Prevalence Rates

The data on the prevalence of cannabis use in Australia over the past twenty years are limited, and the comparability of what is available is uncertain because of changes in the survey methods used. Despite this, the available data from population and school surveys is consistent in suggesting that the prevalence of cannabis use has increased over the past 20 years. The prevalence of cannabis use appears to have increased substantially through the 1970s and early 1980s, levelled off in the late 1980s, and shown a small increase in the early 1990s. ()

The impact of decriminalisation of the personal use of cannabis, or the introduction of civil penalty options for minor cannabis offences — as in South Australia and the ACT — has been investigated in a number of Australian and international jurisdictions.

According to the authors of the relevant commissioned research paper, () problems exist for these studies, as comparisons are made difficult by the fact that civil penalty options have been implemented in different ways, and that good trend data are often lacking for the period from before to after legislative changes were implemented. Further, comparisons between jurisdictions with and without civil penalties for minor cannabis offences are complicated by the fact that many of the jurisdictions which have introduced civil penalty schemes for personal cannabis use had higher rates of use to begin with. ()

An overview of experience in the United States and the Netherlands, completed for the Task Force, () indicates that (a) for the United States, states which introduced systems of civil penalties for cannabis use have not differed from other states in their patterns or trends in cannabis use, and (b) in the Netherlands, where personal cannabis use is dealt with via an administrative "expediency principle", whereby such use is generally permitted to go unpenalised, there does not appear to have been any major changes in the prevalence of cannabis use since the policy of "de facto" decriminalisation was introduced in 1976. ()

In Australia, an evaluation of the effects of South Australia's Cannabis Expiation Notice (CEN) scheme has shown no evidence of any change in cannabis use rates in the few years following the scheme's introduction, which might be attributable to the new laws.

Overall, the available data from different countries suggest that cannabis decriminalisation has not led to increases in cannabis use. Where slight increases in use have been observed in jurisdictions which have adopted civil penalty options, they are of a similar magnitude to the broader increases observed elsewhere.

## Conclusion

In conclusion then; there is in Australia, as in many other countries, a growing swell of rational, reasoned opinion arguing that there needs to be changes in the way in which we, as Australians, deal with the problems posed to our society by the use of illicit drugs, particularly cannabis.

What I have attempted to demonstrate in this paper is that concerned politicians at both the State/Territorial level and the Federal level in Australia, and a concomitant group of concerned citizens, are committed to, and are, influencing governments to change our drug laws to the betterment of our society.
