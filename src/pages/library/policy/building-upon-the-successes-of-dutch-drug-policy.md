*Peter Cohen, Director of the Amsterdam Drug Research Project, University of Amsterdam. Member of the International League against Prohibition.*

To describe the success of Dutch drug policy, I write not only from the perspective of an academic researcher at the Amsterdam Drug Research Programme, but also as a Member of the International League against Prohibition. Consequently, some value judgements may be used that can not claim to be supported by scientific evidence. For these I will utilise political and moral principles that I hope will become clear.

To briefly define the positive consequences of the general Dutch drug policy, I have chosen five aspects which illustrate some of its successes. These are the prevalence of drug use, prevalence of fatal drug accidents, availability of assistance for potential clients, the conditions for the development of rules to control individual drug use and lastly, the prevalence of non drug-related hazards of drug use.

## Prevalence of drug use

For most observers, one of the incredible outcomes of the drug policies in the Netherlands is the relatively low prevalence of drug use, in spite of — or possibly because of — a policy which is not focused on either penalization of individual drug users, or at suppressing drug use by means of media campaigns.

From a household survey, published by NIDA for the year 1988, we know that life time prevalence of marijuana use in the U.S.A. is 33% for the population over 12 years of age. In the Netherlands we have no national household surveys, but from a household survey conducted in Amsterdam during two months in 1987, also of the population of 12 years and older, we know that life time prevalence of marijuana use is 23.7%, which is considerably lower. The percentage of persons who used marijuana in the month prior to interview was 6% for the whole of the U.S.A., compared to 5.7% in the Amsterdam population. Urban drug use, however, is usually far higher than rural and provincial drug use. This means that comparing Amsterdam to all of the U.S.A. (in which all rural areas are included) is methodologically unreliable. With certainty, national figures for the whole of the Netherlands would be far lower than the ones I have cited for Amsterdam alone. But, even if we compare the city to the nation, we still find marijuana use in Amsterdam to be lower than in the whole of the U.S.A., even though cannabis products can be bought freely in Amsterdam, at very low prices without any risk for involvement in criminal activities or with legal institutions.

If we compare the figures for cocaine use, we find similar differences. Looking at previous month use, 1.5% for the U.S.A. as a whole compares to 0.6% for urban Amsterdam. These are minute proportions of our populations, in itself a revealing datum from the perspective of the massive drugs wars launched. The difference becomes even more striking when we take into account that individual cocaine use and low level dealing of cocaine is rarely a concern for the Dutch police. This makes for an easy-going, user-friendly market where violence is rare. In the U.S.A., however, the threat of undercover police operations is ever present and, moreover, the American cocaine market at the user levels is highly criminalised. In spite of the major differences in law enforcement, the lenient Dutch system is associated with considerably lower prevalence levels.

Another revealing factor is what I would term the 'climatological conditions' that surround drug use. In Amsterdam, the use of drugs by individuals is virtually a non-issue, which results in a lower tendency to hide personal drug use. But, at a recent conference in Europe, a distinguished American epidemiologist revealed his fear that household reporting of drug use in the U.S., despite data anonymity, can no longer be considered reliable because of the great social and media pressures that surround the use of illegal drugs. Consequently, it is more than likely that actual levels of illegal drug use are higher in the U.S.A. than reported via household surveys. Even despite this very important difference between the U.S.A. and Amsterdam, all data from the Dutch city reveal much lower levels of drug use.

I witnessed an example of the enormous effect of the drug climate on drug use reporting at a conference in New York last year. At a session on epidemiology of drug use, the moderator asked who had used marijuana in the last year. Only two Europeans raised their hands and none of the Americans. But many Americans raised their hands when asked who thought the question was answered in an unreliable way.

## Fatal drug accidents

Another indication of what one might call a success of Dutch drug policies is the very low incidence of fatal accidents attributed to drug use. In 1988, only 15 of the estimated 4,500 Amsterdam drug addicts were found dead with a high probability of a drug accident. That is one third of one percent. This figure is the lowest in Europe and, although I have no data from similar American cities, I would guess it is lower than anywhere in the United States. However, if we look at the figures for HIV seropositivity among addicts in Amsterdam we find ourselves on much thinner ground. In a recent paper the Dutch researcher Dirk Korf attacked the official figure for HIV seropositivity in Amsterdam, which is a staggering 30%. Korf reasoned that the research sample was unrepresentative, as he estimated the percentage of HIV seropositivity in the Amsterdam population of addicts at about 12%. Although this is still higher than in San Francisco, it compares favourably with the New York figure of over 50%. But once more, the figures from Amsterdam are rough guides and no more.

It is important at this point to note that the Dutch drug control system is deeply enmeshed in a national health care system available to all Dutch citizens. And that drug control is also part of an extensive system of socio-economically based policies resulting in a mix of law enforcement and social assistance institutions, used to manage social conflicts and social inequality. These socio-economic based policies are too complicated to go into here, but the reason I mention them is to make clear that building upon the relative successes of the Dutch drug control system in other national contexts, one should not analyse drug control as a separate item. This means that a state that has little or no socio-economic assistance institutions to play a role in the management of social conflict, the symbolic role of drug control and the techniques used for it, like law enforcement, will probably be fundamentally different than in a state which does have such institutions.

For example, in Sweden, drug prohibitionist-ideology is even more fundamentalist than in the U.S.A. Still, in the Swedish system of basic care open to every citizen, law enforcement plays only a secondary role. Drug law offenders are preferably kept away from the prison system, but are collected in large institutions that the Swedes see as drug treatment. That I see them as vast camps for enforced therapies and disciplinary action is not relevant. What is relevant, however, is that in Sweden drug control can not be executed at gun point as in the U.S.A., because the systems available, and considered appropriate, for dealing with social conflict in general, are different from those in the United States.

## Clients using the drug assistance system

A relatively major success of Dutch drug control is the proportion of potential clients that, in reality, make contact with the drug treatment system. In general, we estimate that at least 70% of all potential clients have relevant contact with drug treatment services of social assistance systems in a city like Amsterdam, and maybe 50% in smaller towns. This large coverage by the care system means that many potential problem cases are seen on a regular basis, which is very important for the prevention of drug related crises. It also reflects that, in Amsterdam for example, the range of assistance programs offered to addicts is quite broad. In the case of methadone treatment, there is a varied and individually adaptable regime of methadone maintenance, ranging from very strict programmes, monitored by urine analysis, to more flexible, low-threshold maintenance systems. Within this range of treatment options, many clients find the programme to suit their needs.

In the U.S.A., there exist more rigid state or federal rules about how to prescribe methadone. Apart from the fact that methadone maintenance is only available to a small proportion of Americans who could use it, such programmes are so rigidly controlled that many treatment personnel feel constrained by the lack of variability in methadone treatment regimes. In the Netherlands, we now know that one cannot expect all clients to fit a rigid rule governed by medical institutions, as it is recognised that to respond effectively to the many different kinds of clients, a range of treatment options is required. No American would tolerate state rules on the amounts and regularity of benzodiazepines or other pharmaceutical drugs that can be prescribed to him or herself or family members. In this respect, contact between treatment personnel and clients is tailored to individual needs. But in drug treatment rigid rules applicable to all, is considered normal in the U.S.A.

Building on the success of the Dutch system means two things — creating a far more varied health care system for those addicts who want to use it than is available at the moment even in Holland, and tuning the care system to the different micro-social problems addicts in a certain national context will take with them to a clinic. Maybe one addict needs only regular injectable methadone, another irregular oral methadone but regular housing, while another needs no methadone at all (he/she copes well in the black market) but this one needs help only with the education of one of his/her children. Making relevant options available to a higher and higher degree will mean one is building and enlarging upon one of the basic theories of present drug treatment in Dutch cities, ensuring low numbers of clients that need no assistance at all, while ensuring a high level of harm reduction and prevention. In many countries this will not be possible. Not because there is no money or no basic social structure, but because drug addicts are considered dangerous and not as quite normal human beings.

## Developing rules to control individual drug use

I do not consider the figures of lower prevalence of illegal drug use in the Netherlands as compared to the U.S.A. a very interesting measure of success. In itself, using drugs is a perfectly human and innocent activity as long as certain controls are in operation. Controls are individually applied rules of use, that prevent the occurrence of accidents. Controls of drug use are comparable to the traffic rules. Driving a car at 50 miles an hour can turn that car into a deadly weapon if some basic rules are not observed. But, as we all know, the vast majority of drivers observe these rules very well.

The same can be said of drugs. The vast majority of drug users have learned the rules of drug use behaviour. Thus, how many drug users a certain country has is far less important than most people think. As long as most drug users control their use, it does not matter if a country has many or few drug users.

This means that a far more important measure of the success or failure of drug control policies is the proportion of cases where loss of control is apparent — not the prevalence of drug use itself. We might then start to investigate how far possible differences in loss of control between countries with different drug policies are related to these policies or to underlying social and economic situations that are not addressed by current drug policies. We have no systematic data about this because researchers are rarely paid or asked to compare data from different drug climatological areas in the world. But again, I am referring here to building upon the successes of the Netherlands. So, once again I will use some illustrations from the Dutch status quo.

Cannabis is easily and cheaply available in most of the larger Dutch cities. The Dutch government allows this because of the belief that most cannabis users are very controlled users. Strangely enough, no one in the Netherlands even thinks one needs research in this matter. This means that cannabis users are not arrested, unlike in the UK, for example, where last year over 30,000 people were arrested because they are cannabis users. Furthermore, we have no cannabis users transported to mental hospitals, as is the case in Germany. The knowledge of how to regulate cannabis use has become an inconspicuous part of local Dutch youth culture, because the development of drug use rules was not pushed out of the mainstream and into deviant subcultures. Building upon this success of Dutch drug policy, one might suggest that similar leeway for the development of control mechanisms for other drugs is given. Let us consider and study the Dutch cannabis tolerance, its resulting non-criminal distribution system and its success in preventing harmful drug use patterns as a model for cocaine and the opiates.

At the moment, it is quite inconceivable for the Dutch government to consider that the success of their cannabis policy could be made to work for cocaine and opiates. The reason for this is the fear of international condemnation and immediate global economic sanctions. This fear is probably exaggerated but, nevertheless, it is deeply rooted. Personally, I would consider it an excellent idea to make the international bodies unite on allowing the Dutch to develop their own natural experiment, instead of having it suffocate in more and more depressing stalemate. The reason for this stalemate is that the global drug war being propagated by the U.S., and the resulting possibilities for ruthless exploitation of public drug-fears for gains in the power game in some European countries, has almost drained the Netherlands of the possibility of further developing its immature but effective system of drug control.

## Avoiding non drug-related risks

Another measure of success might be the avoidance of non drug-related risks of drug use. In the U.S.A., marijuana smokers risk the ingestion of paraquat, while heroin and cocaine users risk the ingestion of a whole spectrum of unhealthy adulterants. This means that persons, many of whom control their drug use, are confronted with risks that have nothing to do with drugs, but with drug policy. Risks related to the enforcement of drug laws may also have nothing to do with drugs per se. Consider the family who see themselves deprived of their home, confiscated by the DEA, because some marijuana plants or some cocaine powder was found on the premises in a 'Zero Tolerance' army raid. This family faces dramatic problems that have nothing to do with drug consumption. This line of thinking — "let us try not to make the harmful effects of drug control more damaging than the possible harmful effects of illegal drugs" — is quite normal in the Netherlands and should be built upon. Let us find out by scientific inquiry which of the harmful effects of drugs are related to drug control policy, and which ones to the drug and its social use context itself. This will be difficult, but it may help to steer irrational drug policies back on the track of rationality.

Another way of building on the successes of Holland might be the perceived necessity of inundating the public with nonsense about drugs. In the U.S. media, TV spots brainwash the population with the association between illegal drugs and death or psychological decay. Not only does the depicted black drug user become an almost legitimate target of social ostracism and discrimination, it also makes the learning of drug use control mechanisms an underground activity, with all its possibilities of error and folklore. In the Netherlands, fortunately, there is no 'Media Advertising Partnership for a Drug Free Society Incorporated' — a vicious coalition between official bureaucracy and the most powerful media in the world — which kills each and every attempt to acquire real knowledge, to ask for nuance and to ask for rationality. Americans are now used to it. But such media campaigns are, in my simple Dutch eyes, nothing less than a McCarthyian suppression of common sense, intellectual integrity and human rights.

Dutch drug policy has not created these kinds of social and political ulcers. Let's build upon this success by exposing this present so-called drug information as a dangerous backfiring gun. Because once the public discovers how nonsensical it is, good drug information will also be rejected. Consequently, possibilities for honest and decent information will become extremely meagre.

## Questioning drugspeak

The final aspect of Dutch drug policy I will address, is its emerging position as a nation that, in international associations, has started to ask for data and for reasons. Recently, the U.S.A has been trying to sell the latest drugspeak to the Europeans, with a particular emphasis on cocaine. Many Dutch officials look at this with distaste and have reacted with their own arguments, not only in the Council of Europe and the EEC but also to the American Ambassador in the Hague. This has, in part, been quite successful. Not only did the American Ambassador become a deeply impressed supporter of Dutch drug policies, but national data searches were proposed which were heavily influenced by Dutch attempts to put some reason and data before rhetoric. I can not stress enough the importance of the behaviour of large international bodies. Up till now they acted as the divine global executioners of — mostly American instigated — debilitating international treaties. Only when state representatives in the working committees of these bodies are no longer selected from the blind and faithful, can some of the real world enter these bureaucratic fortresses.

We do need some fresh air in these international bodies, otherwise nations that simply ask questions and request data will have no chance. This means that researchers intent on change in the drug control system will have to do the work. To expose these bodies as Ivory Towers developing a stone-age ideology, "Prohibitionism", that has become a real material force. It not only kills thousands of people each year, it has given birth to the largest criminal network the world has ever seen. But far more serious is the possibility that Prohibitionism becomes a vehicle for fascism, as many drug workers in the U.S. have pointed out to me. Some Dutch state officials in some international bodies are angrily asking questions in the face of fundamentalism and rhetoric. Let us build upon that.
