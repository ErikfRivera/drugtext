The LaGuardia Committee Report is one of the most significant early scientific investigations into the effects of marijuana use in the United States. Commissioned by New York City Mayor Fiorello H. LaGuardia, the Mayor's Committee on Marihuana — comprising physicians, pharmacologists, psychologists, sociologists, and law enforcement specialists — conducted a comprehensive multi-year study of marijuana use in New York City, publishing their findings in 1944.

The report challenged many prevailing myths about marijuana, including its supposed link to violent crime and addiction, and remains a landmark document in the history of drug policy.

## About This Report

The Mayor's Committee on Marihuana was convened in 1938 at the request of Mayor LaGuardia, who had grown skeptical of sensationalized claims about marijuana circulating in the press and in federal drug policy discourse. The committee included some of the leading scientific minds in New York City: staff from the New York Academy of Medicine, psychiatrists and clinical psychologists from Bellevue Hospital, pharmacologists, and representatives from the New York City Police Department.

The Committee conducted both sociological fieldwork — documenting patterns of marijuana use in Harlem and other New York neighborhoods — and controlled clinical studies on volunteers, examining the drug's effects on physiology, cognition, emotional functioning, and social behavior. The pharmacological study analyzed the active constituents of marijuana preparations.

The report's conclusions were notably at odds with the dominant federal narrative: the Committee found no evidence that marijuana use was widespread among schoolchildren, that it led to heroin addiction, or that it caused violent crime. The report documented that marijuana produced mild euphoria, reduced anxiety, and impaired coordination, but did not produce the severe moral and physical deterioration claimed by prohibitionists.

The LaGuardia Report drew hostile responses from the Federal Bureau of Narcotics under Harry Anslinger, who dismissed it as unscientific. It nonetheless stands as a foundational text in evidence-based drug policy, anticipating many conclusions reached by later commissions decades afterward.
