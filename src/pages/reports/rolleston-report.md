*Departmental Committee on Morphine and Heroin Addiction, United Kingdom (1926).*

## About This Report

The Rolleston Report is the 1926 report of the United Kingdom's Departmental Committee on Morphine and Heroin Addiction, commonly named after its chairman Sir Humphry Rolleston. Commissioned in the wake of the Dangerous Drugs Act 1920, the Committee examined the circumstances under which physicians might legitimately prescribe morphine or heroin to patients suffering from addiction.

The report concluded that addiction to morphine or heroin was a disease condition, and that maintenance prescribing by a physician could in certain cases be the appropriate medical treatment. This framework — often called the **Rolleston System** — underpinned British drug policy for decades and stands in marked contrast to the American prohibitionist approach of the same era.

A full PDF of the report is held in the Drugtext archive (availability subject to confirmation).

## Contents

The report is divided into eight sections:

1. Introduction and Minutes
2. Section I — Preliminary Observations
3. Section II — Medical Aspects of the Addiction Problem
4. Section III — Circumstances in Which It May Be Medically Advisable to Administer Morphine or Heroin to Persons Known to Be Suffering from Addiction to These Drugs
5. Section IV — Precautions to Be Observed in the Administration of Morphine or Heroin
6. Section V — Administrative Measures
7. Section VI — Preparations at Present Excluded from the Scope of the Dangerous Drugs Acts
8. Conclusions and Recommendations

## Historical Significance

The Rolleston Report established what became known as the "British System" of addiction management — a medically supervised, maintenance-based approach treating addiction as a health matter rather than a criminal one. Its influence persisted until the mid-1960s, when the Brain Committee (First Brain Report 1961; Second Brain Report 1965) reviewed the system in response to new patterns of heroin misuse and recommended tighter controls.

For a detailed account of how the Rolleston framework shaped, and was eventually challenged by, evolving conceptions of the addict in British policy, see the article [Changing Images of the Addict and Addiction](/library/addiction/changing-images-of-the-addict) on this site.
